About Physics Logic

Transforming how people learn physics by placing logic, reasoning, and deep understanding at the center of every lesson.

Our Mission

Physics is not a collection of formulas to memorize. It is a way of thinking—a disciplined practice of observing the natural world, asking precise questions, and reasoning toward answers that withstand the scrutiny of experiment. Yet for too many students, physics education has become exactly the opposite: a cycle of memorizing equations, substituting numbers, and moving on without ever understanding why those equations hold true.

Physics Logic Lectures exists to break that cycle. Our mission is to make physics deeply, genuinely understandable by showing the logical structure behind every concept. We believe that when a student sees how Newton arrived at his laws through careful reasoning, or how Maxwell unified electricity and magnetism through mathematical logic, the formulas cease to be arbitrary symbols and become inevitable conclusions. Understanding replaces memorization, and real learning begins.

We are building a free, comprehensive resource that takes students from the very foundations of physical reasoning to the frontiers of modern physics—not by cutting corners or oversimplifying, but by illuminating the logical threads that connect every idea to every other idea in the magnificent tapestry of physical law.

Teaching Philosophy

Our approach rests on a simple conviction: concepts come before equations, intuition before formulas, and understanding why before learning how. Every lecture begins not with a formula, but with a question. Why do objects fall? Why does light bend? Why does energy never simply vanish? From these questions, we build the conceptual scaffolding that gives mathematical expressions their meaning.

Consider how most textbooks introduce kinetic energy. They present the formula KE = ½mv² and then demonstrate it through worked examples. Our approach is different. We begin by asking what it means for a moving object to carry the capacity to cause change. We explore how force applied over a distance transfers something measurable from one system to another. We derive the relationship between mass, velocity, and that transferred quantity step by step, so that by the time the formula appears on the page, it feels not like a fact handed down from authority, but like an insight the student has arrived at independently through their own reasoning.

This philosophy extends to every topic we cover. Derivations are never skipped or relegated to appendices—they are the main event. We show every logical step, explain every assumption, and highlight every point where a different assumption would lead to a different result. We also take time to address common misconceptions head-on, because understanding what a concept does not mean is just as important as understanding what it does.

The result is a learning experience that builds genuine physical intuition. Students who work through these lectures do not merely know that F = ma; they understand what force, mass, and acceleration truly mean, why they relate to one another in that particular way, and where the boundaries of that relationship lie. This depth of understanding is what separates students who can solve problems from students who can think like physicists.

How the Lecture System Works

Each lecture in the Physics Logic series follows a carefully designed structure that supports deep learning at every stage. A lecture is not simply a wall of text—it is an organized journey through a topic, built from components that each serve a specific pedagogical purpose.

Every lecture opens with an overview that frames the topic in context: what question we are trying to answer, how it connects to what came before, and why it matters. This is followed by clearly stated learning goals, so students know from the outset what they should be able to do and understand by the end.

The core of each lecture consists of detailed explanations organized into logical sections. Within these sections, you will find several recurring elements:

  • Definitions that establish precise language, because clear thinking requires clear terminology.
  • Derivations that show every step of reasoning from assumptions to conclusions, with commentary explaining the logic behind each step.
  • Worked examples that demonstrate how concepts apply to concrete problems, with solutions that model expert problem-solving strategies rather than mechanical formula application.
  • Misconception alerts that identify and correct the most common errors in understanding, helping students avoid the conceptual traps that derail so many learners.
  • Summaries that consolidate the key ideas and prepare the reader for the next lecture in the sequence.

The lectures are designed to build on each other in a deliberate logical progression. Later lectures reference and deepen ideas introduced earlier, creating a web of interconnected understanding. This means that a student who works through the entire series will emerge not just with knowledge of individual topics, but with a coherent, unified picture of how the physical world operates.

Who This Is For

Physics Logic Lectures is designed to serve anyone with the curiosity to understand how the physical world works and the willingness to think carefully. In particular, we have built this resource with four audiences in mind:

High School Students

Building strong, correct foundations from the very beginning. These lectures give you the deep understanding that will make advanced courses easier and more rewarding. Rather than learning physics as a set of recipes for solving textbook problems, you will develop genuine physical intuition that stays with you.

University Students

Deepening your understanding beyond what standard textbooks provide. If you have ever felt that you could solve the homework but did not truly understand the material, these lectures will fill in the conceptual gaps. They are especially valuable as a companion to formal coursework, offering the "why" behind the "what" that lectures often move through too quickly.

Self-Learners

Exploring physics independently on your own schedule. Whether you are a professional in another field who has always been fascinated by physics, a lifelong learner seeking intellectual challenge, or someone returning to a subject you once studied, these lectures are written to be self-contained and accessible without a classroom or instructor.

Competitors

Preparing for physics olympiads, competitions, and advanced examinations. The emphasis on deep conceptual understanding and rigorous logical reasoning is exactly what competition-level physics demands. These lectures help you develop the flexible, creative thinking that distinguishes top performers from students who rely on pattern-matching alone.

The Journey

The Physics Logic lecture series is a carefully sequenced arc of twelve lectures that takes you from the very foundations of physical reasoning to the ideas that define modern physics. The journey begins with Lecture 1: Introduction to Physics and Physical Reasoning, where we establish what physics is, how observation leads to models and laws, and what it means to think logically about the natural world. From there, Lecture 2 builds the essential language of measurement—units, dimensions, precision, and uncertainty—because rigorous reasoning requires rigorous tools.

Lectures 3 through 7 form the heart of classical mechanics. We move from vectors and motion through Newton's laws, energy and momentum, and onward to circular motion and universal gravitation. Each lecture builds directly on the previous one: you cannot deeply understand energy without first understanding force, and you cannot grasp gravitation without both. This is not an arbitrary ordering—it is the logical structure of mechanics itself.

Lecture 8 bridges mechanics into the world of waves and oscillations, revealing how the same principles that govern a swinging pendulum also explain sound, light, and the behavior of matter at every scale. Lecture 9 takes us into thermodynamics, where the concept of energy reaches its full generality and we encounter entropy—perhaps the most profound idea in all of physics.

Lectures 10 and 11 explore electricity, magnetism, and optics, showing how seemingly separate phenomena are deeply connected through the electromagnetic field. Finally, Lecture 12: Modern Physics draws the entire journey together by introducing relativity and quantum mechanics—ideas that challenged and ultimately expanded everything that came before, and that continue to shape our understanding of the universe today.

Each lecture is a complete, self-contained lesson, but the real power of the series emerges when you experience the full arc. By the end, you will not just know physics; you will understand it as a single, interconnected system of ideas—built from logic, tested by experiment, and open to the next great question.
