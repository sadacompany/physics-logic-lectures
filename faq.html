Frequently Asked Questions

Everything you need to know about Physics Logic Lectures, from getting started to making the most of each topic.

Physics Logic Lectures is a free, self-paced educational platform that teaches physics through the lens of logical reasoning and first-principles thinking. Each lecture is carefully structured to build not just knowledge of physical laws, but the ability to derive and apply those laws through clear, step-by-step reasoning.

The platform is designed for high school students, university freshmen, and curious self-learners who want a solid foundation in physics. Whether you are preparing for exams, supplementing your coursework, or simply exploring the subject for the first time, the lectures are written to be accessible and engaging for a wide range of backgrounds.

No prior physics knowledge is required. The lecture series begins with foundational concepts and builds progressively, so you can start from scratch. A basic familiarity with algebra and simple arithmetic is helpful, but each mathematical concept is explained as it arises.

Each lecture follows a consistent format: an introduction that frames the core question, a guided exploration of the key ideas with real-world examples, and a summary with key takeaways. The 12-lecture series is grouped into categories such as Foundations, Mechanics, Waves, Thermal Physics, Electricity and Magnetism, and Modern Physics.

Unlike a traditional textbook, Physics Logic Lectures emphasizes the reasoning process over memorization of formulas. The conversational tone guides you through each concept as though you are discovering it yourself. Diagrams, analogies, and thought experiments are used throughout to make abstract ideas tangible and intuitive.

We recommend following the lectures in order, starting from Lecture 1, since each one builds on ideas introduced in earlier sessions. Take your time with each topic, revisit sections that feel challenging, and try to work through the thought experiments on your own before reading the explanations. Active engagement is far more effective than passive reading.

These are designed to test your reasoning rather than your ability to plug numbers into equations. We encourage you to spend additional time reflecting on the key ideas and revisiting earlier lectures as needed. There is no time pressure, so learn at the pace that works best for you.

Together, they form a comprehensive first course in physics.
