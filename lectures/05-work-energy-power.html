Learning Goals

  • Define work precisely and understand why the angle between force and displacement matters
  • Distinguish between positive, negative, and zero work
  • Derive kinetic energy from the work-energy theorem
  • State and apply the work-energy theorem to solve problems

Overview

In the previous lectures, we studied forces and Newton's laws. We learned how to analyze what happens when forces act on objects: how objects accelerate, how they interact through action-reaction pairs, and how to apply free-body diagrams to solve problems. That framework is enormously powerful, but it has a limitation. Forces and accelerations are vectors. They have directions, and keeping track of those directions in complex situations—objects sliding along curved surfaces, pendulums swinging, roller coasters looping—can become extraordinarily difficult.

This lecture introduces a fundamentally different approach to mechanics, one that does not replace Newton's laws but complements them with a tool of remarkable elegance. Instead of tracking forces and accelerations at every instant, we will learn to track energy: a single scalar quantity that is transferred, transformed, and conserved. The energy approach lets us answer questions like "how fast will this object be moving at the bottom of the hill?" without ever solving a differential equation or decomposing a single vector.

We begin with the concept of work, which is the mechanism by which energy is transferred from one system to another. From work, we derive kinetic energy—the energy of motion—and the work-energy theorem, which connects them. We then introduce potential energy, both gravitational and elastic, and show that when only conservative forces act, the total mechanical energy of a system is conserved. This conservation principle is one of the most powerful ideas in all of physics. It extends far beyond mechanics into thermodynamics, electromagnetism, quantum mechanics, and every other branch of the discipline. Understanding it deeply here will serve you for the rest of your physics education and career.

Finally, we discuss power: the rate at which work is done or energy is transferred. Power tells us not just how much energy is involved, but how quickly it is being delivered, which is critical in engineering, biology, and everyday life. An elevator motor, a sprinting athlete, and a charging smartphone all involve power, and understanding this concept allows us to analyze all of them.

The Concept of Work

In everyday language, "work" means effort. If you carry a heavy box across a room, you feel like you are doing work. If you push against a wall until your arms ache, you feel like you are doing work. But in physics, work has a precise, quantitative definition that sometimes disagrees with our intuitive sense. Understanding this definition—and why physics defines it this way—is the first step toward mastering energy.

The physics definition of work captures a specific idea: work is done on an object when a force causes that object to move through a displacement. The key word is "causes." If you push against a wall and the wall does not move, you have exerted a force, but you have done no work on the wall in the physics sense. Your muscles are contracting and consuming metabolic energy, yes, but no energy has been transferred to the wall. The wall's kinetic energy has not changed. Its potential energy has not changed. Nothing about the wall's mechanical state has changed. Physics reserves the word "work" for situations in which energy is actually transferred.

Now, consider the case where the force and the displacement are not in the same direction. Suppose you pull a sled with a rope that makes an angle with the ground. Only the component of your pulling force that is along the direction of the sled's motion contributes to speeding it up or slowing it down. The component of force perpendicular to the motion does not contribute to the displacement—it merely presses the sled into the ground or lifts it slightly. This leads us to the precise definition of work:

Work Done by a Constant Force W = F d cos θ

Here, F is the magnitude of the force, d is the magnitude of the displacement, and θ is the angle between the force vector and the displacement vector. The cos θ factor is what makes this definition powerful and precise. It automatically accounts for the direction of the force relative to the motion.

Work (W)

The product of the component of force along the direction of displacement and the magnitude of the displacement. Measured in joules (J), where 1 J = 1 N · m. Work is a scalar quantity—it has magnitude but no direction.

Why the Angle Matters

The angle θ in the work formula is not a minor detail. It determines the very sign of the work, which tells us whether energy is being added to or removed from the object. Let us consider three important cases:

Case 1: θ = 0° (force parallel to displacement). The force is in the same direction as the motion. cos 0° = 1, so W = Fd. This is the maximum possible work for a given force and displacement. Example: you push a box across the floor in the direction it is moving. The work is positive, meaning you are transferring energy to the box.

Case 2: θ = 90° (force perpendicular to displacement). cos 90° = 0, so W = 0. A force perpendicular to the motion does zero work. This is one of the most counterintuitive results in introductory physics. A person carrying a box horizontally at constant height exerts an upward force to support the box, but the displacement is horizontal. The angle between the upward force and the horizontal displacement is 90°, so the work done by the carrying force is zero. Similarly, the normal force on an object sliding along a flat surface does zero work, because the normal force is perpendicular to the surface and the displacement is along the surface.

Case 3: θ = 180° (force opposite to displacement). cos 180° = −1, so W = −Fd. The work is negative. This means the force is removing energy from the object. The most familiar example is kinetic friction: the friction force always opposes the motion, so friction always does negative work on a sliding object, reducing its kinetic energy.

Key Concept

Positive work means energy is transferred to the object, increasing its kinetic energy. Negative work means energy is transferred from the object, decreasing its kinetic energy. Zero work means no energy transfer occurs, even if a force is being exerted.

Worked Example

A person pulls a 15 kg sled along flat ground with a rope that makes a 30° angle above the horizontal. The tension in the rope is 40 N, and the sled moves 20 m. How much work does the person do on the sled?

Solution

The force is F = 40 N, the displacement is d = 20 m, and the angle between them is θ = 30°.

W = Fd cos θ = (40)(20) cos 30° = 800 × 0.866 = 693 J

The person does 693 joules of work on the sled. Notice that this is less than 800 J (which would be the work if the force were entirely horizontal) because the vertical component of the tension does not contribute to horizontal displacement.

Work Done by Multiple Forces

When multiple forces act on an object, each force does its own work. The total work done on the object is the sum of the work done by each individual force. Alternatively, you can find the net force first and then calculate the work done by the net force. Both approaches yield the same result. The total (net) work is what determines how the object's kinetic energy changes, as we will see in the work-energy theorem.

Consider a box being pushed across a rough floor. The applied force does positive work (it is in the direction of motion). The friction force does negative work (it opposes the motion). The normal force and gravity do zero work (they are perpendicular to the motion on a flat surface). The net work is the algebraic sum of all these contributions.

Kinetic Energy

Now that we have defined work, we can ask: when net work is done on an object, what exactly changes? The answer is the object's kinetic energy. Kinetic energy is the energy an object possesses by virtue of its motion. A moving car has kinetic energy. A thrown baseball has kinetic energy. Even air molecules zipping around at hundreds of meters per second have kinetic energy (their collective kinetic energy is what we perceive as temperature, but that is a story for a later lecture on thermodynamics).

The expression for kinetic energy can be derived directly from Newton's second law and the definition of work. Consider an object of mass m, initially at rest, acted on by a constant net force F. The object accelerates uniformly with acceleration a = F/m. After traveling a distance d, its velocity v can be found from the kinematic equation v² = 2ad, which gives d = v²/(2a). The work done by the net force is:

W = Fd = ma · v²/(2a) = ½mv²

This is the kinetic energy. The net work done on the object equals the kinetic energy it gains. This derivation reveals that kinetic energy is not an arbitrary definition. It is the natural quantity that emerges when you ask, "How much work does it take to bring an object from rest to speed v?"

Kinetic Energy KE = ½mv²
Kinetic Energy (KE)

The energy of motion. For an object of mass m moving at speed v, KE = ½mv². Kinetic energy is always non-negative (zero when at rest, positive when moving). It is a scalar quantity measured in joules (J).

Notice the v² dependence. If you double an object's speed, its kinetic energy quadruples. This has profound practical consequences. A car traveling at 120 km/h has four times the kinetic energy of the same car at 60 km/h. This is why high-speed crashes are so much more destructive than low-speed ones: the energy that must be dissipated in the collision scales with the square of the velocity, not linearly with it. A car going twice as fast does not need twice the stopping distance—it needs four times the stopping distance (assuming the same braking force).

Also notice the linear dependence on mass. A truck with twice the mass of a car, traveling at the same speed, has twice the kinetic energy. Mass matters, but speed matters more. This is why even a small bullet, moving at hundreds of meters per second, can have devastating kinetic energy.

Key Concept

Kinetic energy depends on the square of velocity. Doubling the speed quadruples the kinetic energy. This v² dependence explains why high-speed impacts are far more destructive than low-speed ones and why stopping distance increases quadratically with speed.

The Work-Energy Theorem

The derivation above was for the special case of an object starting from rest. But the result generalizes beautifully. For any object, regardless of its initial velocity, the net work done on it equals the change in its kinetic energy:

Work-Energy Theorem Wnet = ΔKE = ½mvf² − ½mvi²

This is the work-energy theorem, and it is one of the most important results in classical mechanics. Let us unpack what it says. The left side, Wnet, is the total work done on the object by all forces combined. The right side, ΔKE, is the change in kinetic energy: final kinetic energy minus initial kinetic energy. The theorem tells us that these two quantities are always equal.

If the net work is positive, the kinetic energy increases, and the object speeds up. If the net work is negative, the kinetic energy decreases, and the object slows down. If the net work is zero, the kinetic energy does not change, and the speed remains constant (even though individual forces may be doing nonzero work that cancels out).

The power of the work-energy theorem lies in the fact that work and kinetic energy are scalars. You do not need to track directions. You do not need to decompose vectors into components. You simply compute the net work (which may involve summing contributions from several forces) and set it equal to the change in kinetic energy. This is often much simpler than applying Newton's second law directly, especially when the motion is along a curved path or when the forces vary with position.

Worked Example

A 1,200 kg car traveling at 25 m/s applies its brakes and comes to a stop over a distance of 60 m. What is the average braking force?

Solution

Using the work-energy theorem: Wnet = ΔKE

ΔKE = ½(1200)(0)² − ½(1200)(25)² = 0 − 375,000 = −375,000 J

The work done by the braking force: W = Fd cos 180° = −F(60)

Setting them equal: −60F = −375,000

F = 6,250 N

The average braking force is 6,250 N. Notice how the work-energy theorem gives us the answer directly without needing to find the acceleration first. We could have used kinematics and Newton's second law to get the same result, but the energy approach is more direct.

Key Concept

The work-energy theorem connects force, displacement, and speed in a single scalar equation. It is a direct consequence of Newton's second law but is often easier to apply because it avoids the need for vector decomposition. The net work done on an object always equals its change in kinetic energy, no matter how complicated the path or forces involved.

Potential Energy

Kinetic energy is the energy of motion. But energy can also be stored in a system by virtue of the configuration of its parts. A book held above the floor has the potential to fall and gain kinetic energy. A compressed spring has the potential to expand and push an object. This stored energy is called potential energy, and it arises whenever a conservative force (gravity, elastic forces, electric forces) is present.

The idea behind potential energy is elegant. When you lift a book from the floor to a shelf, you do work against gravity. That work does not vanish into nothing. It is stored in the Earth-book system as gravitational potential energy. When you release the book, gravity does work on it, converting that stored potential energy back into kinetic energy as the book accelerates downward. The total energy—kinetic plus potential—remains constant throughout the process (assuming no air resistance or other non-conservative forces). This is the core idea of energy conservation, which we will develop formally in the next section.

A critical point that many students miss: potential energy is a property of a system, not of a single object. When we say "the book has gravitational potential energy," we are really saying that the Earth-book system has gravitational potential energy due to the gravitational interaction between the Earth and the book. The energy is stored in the gravitational field between them, not inside the book itself. This distinction becomes important in more advanced physics, but even at this level, it helps to remember that potential energy requires an interaction between objects.

Key Concept

Potential energy is energy stored in a system due to the positions or configurations of its parts. It is associated with conservative forces and represents the capacity to do work. When a conservative force does positive work, potential energy decreases and kinetic energy increases, and vice versa.

Gravitational Potential Energy

The most familiar form of potential energy is gravitational potential energy near the surface of the Earth. When you lift an object of mass m through a height h against gravity, the work you do against gravity is:

W = mgh

This work is stored as gravitational potential energy. If you release the object, gravity will do this same amount of work on it as it falls through height h, converting the potential energy back into kinetic energy.

Gravitational Potential Energy (near Earth's surface) PEgrav = mgh

Here, m is the mass of the object, g is the acceleration due to gravity (approximately 9.8 m/s² near Earth's surface), and h is the height above a chosen reference level. The choice of reference level is arbitrary. You can set h = 0 at the floor, at the table, at sea level, or anywhere else that is convenient. What matters physically is never the absolute value of potential energy, but only the change in potential energy, and that change is the same regardless of where you place the reference level.

Gravitational Potential Energy

The energy stored in a system due to the gravitational interaction between an object and the Earth (or another massive body). Near the Earth's surface, PE = mgh, where h is the height above a chosen reference point. Only changes in PE are physically meaningful; the reference level is arbitrary.

Why is the reference level arbitrary? Because when we use energy conservation, we always compute differences in potential energy. If you choose the floor as h = 0 and the table as h = 1 m, the change in PE when an object falls from the table to the floor is mg(0) − mg(1) = −mg. If instead you choose the table as h = 0, then the floor is at h = −1 m, and the change is mg(−1) − mg(0) = −mg. The change is the same either way. The absolute values differ, but the differences do not.

Worked Example

A 0.5 kg ball is dropped from rest at a height of 12 m above the ground. Ignoring air resistance, how fast is it moving just before it hits the ground?

Solution

Using energy conservation (setting ground as h = 0):

KEi + PEi = KEf + PEf

0 + mgh = ½mv² + 0

mgh = ½mv²

v = √(2gh) = √(2 × 9.8 × 12) = √(235.2) = 15.3 m/s

Notice that the mass cancelled out. The speed at the bottom does not depend on the mass of the ball. This is consistent with what Galileo demonstrated centuries ago: all objects fall at the same rate (in the absence of air resistance). The energy approach gives us this result effortlessly.

Elastic Potential Energy

Springs and other elastic objects can also store potential energy. When you compress or stretch a spring from its natural (equilibrium) length, you do work against the spring force. That work is stored as elastic potential energy. When you release the spring, it converts that stored energy back into kinetic energy of whatever it is pushing.

For an ideal spring that obeys Hooke's law (F = −kx, where k is the spring constant and x is the displacement from equilibrium), the elastic potential energy is:

Elastic Potential Energy PEelastic = ½kx²

Here, k is the spring constant (a measure of the spring's stiffness, in N/m) and x is the displacement from the spring's equilibrium position. Notice that x is squared, so the potential energy is always positive whether the spring is compressed (x < 0) or stretched (x > 0). This makes physical sense: a compressed spring and a stretched spring both have the ability to do work when released.

The ½kx² formula can be derived by calculating the work done to compress (or stretch) the spring from x = 0 to some displacement x. Because the spring force increases linearly with displacement, the average force over the displacement is ½kx, and the work is average force times displacement: W = (½kx)(x) = ½kx². This is the area under the force-versus-displacement graph, a triangle with base x and height kx.

Elastic Potential Energy

The energy stored in a deformed elastic object, such as a compressed or stretched spring. For an ideal spring obeying Hooke's law, PE = ½kx², where k is the spring constant and x is the displacement from equilibrium.

Worked Example

A spring with spring constant k = 800 N/m is compressed by 0.15 m and then releases a 0.2 kg ball. What is the speed of the ball as it leaves the spring?

Solution

The elastic PE stored in the spring converts entirely to KE of the ball:

½kx² = ½mv²

v = x√(k/m) = 0.15 × √(800/0.2) = 0.15 × √4000 = 0.15 × 63.2 = 9.5 m/s

The ball leaves the spring at approximately 9.5 m/s. Again, the energy approach makes this straightforward: we did not need to track the force at every point as the spring decompressed. We simply equated the initial stored energy to the final kinetic energy.

Conservation of Energy

We are now ready to state one of the most profound principles in all of physics: the law of conservation of energy. In a system where only conservative forces do work, the total mechanical energy—the sum of kinetic energy and potential energy—remains constant:

Conservation of Mechanical Energy KE1 + PE1 = KE2 + PE2

This equation says that whatever kinetic energy the system loses, it gains back in potential energy, and vice versa. Energy is not created or destroyed—it is transformed from one form to another. When a ball is thrown upward, kinetic energy is converted into gravitational potential energy as the ball slows and rises. At the highest point, all the kinetic energy has been converted to potential energy. As the ball falls back down, the conversion reverses. At every point along the trajectory, the total mechanical energy is the same.

The conservation of energy is not merely a computational tool (though it is a powerful one). It represents a deep truth about the structure of the physical world. The physicist Emmy Noether proved in 1918 that energy conservation is a direct consequence of the fact that the laws of physics do not change with time. As long as the rules of nature are the same today as they were yesterday and will be tomorrow, energy must be conserved. This connection between symmetry and conservation laws is one of the most beautiful results in theoretical physics.

The Roller Coaster: A Detailed Example

A roller coaster is the classic illustration of energy conservation in mechanics. Consider a roller coaster car of mass m = 500 kg that starts from rest at the top of a hill of height h1 = 40 m. It then descends to a valley at ground level before climbing a second hill of height h2 = 25 m. We want to find the speed at the bottom of the valley and at the top of the second hill, assuming no friction.

Worked Example — Roller Coaster

A 500 kg roller coaster car starts from rest at h1 = 40 m. Find its speed at (a) the bottom of the valley (h = 0) and (b) the top of the second hill (h2 = 25 m).

Solution

(a) At the bottom (h = 0):

KEtop + PEtop = KEbottom + PEbottom

0 + mgh1 = ½mv² + 0

v = √(2gh1) = √(2 × 9.8 × 40) = √784 = 28 m/s

(b) At the second hill (h2 = 25 m):

mgh1 = ½mv² + mgh2

v = √(2g(h1 − h2)) = √(2 × 9.8 × 15) = √294 = 17.1 m/s

Notice again that the mass cancels. The speed depends only on the height difference, not on the mass of the car. Also notice how simple this is compared to using Newton's second law along the curved track. We would need to know the exact shape of the track to apply F = ma at every point, but the energy method skips all of that. It only cares about the starting and ending heights.

Key Concept

Conservation of mechanical energy allows us to relate speeds and heights without knowing the details of the path. This is the great advantage of the energy approach: it connects the initial and final states directly, bypassing the complex details of what happens in between. As long as only conservative forces act, the total mechanical energy is constant.

Conservative vs Non-Conservative Forces

We have used the phrase "conservative force" several times. It is time to define it precisely. A conservative force is one for which the work done on an object depends only on the initial and final positions of the object, not on the path taken between them. Equivalently, a conservative force does zero net work on any object that moves along a closed path (returning to its starting point).

Gravity is a conservative force. If you carry a book from the floor to a shelf, the work done by gravity is −mgh, regardless of whether you lift it straight up, carry it diagonally, or take a winding path through the house. Only the vertical displacement matters. If you then bring the book back to the floor, the total work done by gravity over the round trip is zero.

The elastic spring force is also conservative. The work done by a spring depends only on the initial and final displacements from equilibrium, not on how the spring got there.

Conservative Force

A force for which the work done depends only on the initial and final positions, not on the path taken. Equivalently, the net work done by a conservative force on any closed path is zero. Gravity and elastic spring forces are conservative. Every conservative force has an associated potential energy.

A non-conservative force is one for which the work does depend on the path. Friction is the prime example. If you slide a box from point A to point B along a short straight path, friction does a certain amount of negative work. If you instead slide the box along a long, winding path between the same two points, friction does more negative work (because friction acts over a greater distance). The work done by friction depends on the path, not just the endpoints.

When non-conservative forces are present, mechanical energy is no longer conserved. Friction, for instance, converts mechanical energy into thermal energy (heat). The total energy of the universe is still conserved—the energy has not disappeared—but it has been transformed into a form (heat) that cannot easily be recovered as mechanical energy. The modified energy equation becomes:

Energy with Non-Conservative Forces KE1 + PE1 + Wnc = KE2 + PE2

Here, Wnc is the work done by non-conservative forces. For friction, this is always negative, meaning it removes mechanical energy from the system. The "lost" mechanical energy appears as thermal energy, sound, deformation, or other non-mechanical forms.

Key Concept

Non-conservative forces like friction convert mechanical energy into other forms (primarily heat). The total energy of the universe is always conserved, but mechanical energy alone is conserved only when non-conservative forces do no work. When friction is present, you must account for the energy it removes using Wnc.

Power

Energy tells us how much work is done; power tells us how fast that work is done. Two motors might each lift a 1,000 kg elevator through a height of 20 m, doing the same total work. But if one does it in 10 seconds and the other in 60 seconds, the first motor is six times more powerful. Power is defined as the rate at which work is done (or, equivalently, the rate at which energy is transferred):

Power P = W / t

The SI unit of power is the watt (W), named after James Watt, where 1 W = 1 J/s. A 100-watt light bulb converts 100 joules of electrical energy into light and heat every second. A human performing sustained physical activity can produce about 75–100 W of useful mechanical power, though short bursts (like sprinting or jumping) can briefly reach 1,000–2,000 W.

There is another extremely useful expression for power. If a constant force F acts on an object moving at velocity v, and the force is in the same direction as the velocity, then the work done in a small time interval dt is dW = F · dx = F · v · dt. Therefore:

Instantaneous Power P = Fv cos θ

This tells us that power is the product of force and velocity (with the appropriate angle factor). A car engine must provide more power to maintain the same speed going uphill than on flat ground, because the force required is greater. At highway speeds, the engine must overcome air resistance (which increases with v²), meaning the power required to maintain a constant speed increases with v³—a dramatic dependence that is why fuel economy drops sharply at high speeds.

Power (P)

The rate at which work is done or energy is transferred. Average power is P = W/t. Instantaneous power is P = Fv cos θ. The SI unit is the watt (W), where 1 W = 1 J/s. Another common unit is the horsepower (hp), where 1 hp ≈ 746 W.

Worked Example

An elevator motor lifts a 1,500 kg elevator at a constant speed of 3 m/s. What power must the motor supply?

Solution

At constant velocity, the net force is zero. The motor's tension force must equal the weight: F = mg = (1500)(9.8) = 14,700 N.

Since the force and velocity are in the same direction (θ = 0):

P = Fv = (14,700)(3) = 44,100 W = 44.1 kW

In horsepower: P = 44,100 / 746 ≈ 59 hp. This is the continuous power output required. In practice, the motor must provide additional power to accelerate the elevator from rest, but once at constant speed, 44.1 kW sustains the motion.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception

"If I exert a force, I must be doing work."

Not necessarily. Work requires both a force and a displacement in the direction of that force. If you push against a wall and the wall does not move, you do zero work on the wall. If you carry a box horizontally at constant height, the upward force you exert is perpendicular to the horizontal displacement, so you do zero work on the box (in the physics sense). Your muscles consume metabolic energy, but that energy goes into heat within your body, not into the mechanical energy of the box.

Misconception

"Heavier objects fall faster, so they must have more kinetic energy at the bottom."

Heavier objects do have more kinetic energy at the bottom (KE = ½mv², and m is larger), but they do not fall faster. In the absence of air resistance, all objects fall with the same acceleration g, and objects dropped from the same height reach the ground at the same speed, regardless of mass. The mass cancels in the energy conservation equation: mgh = ½mv² gives v = √(2gh), independent of m. The heavier object has more KE because it had more PE to begin with (PE = mgh), not because it falls faster.

Misconception

"Energy is used up when you do work."

Energy is never used up. It is conserved. When we say an engine "uses" fuel, what we mean is that it converts the chemical potential energy of the fuel into other forms of energy: kinetic energy of the vehicle, thermal energy from friction and exhaust, sound energy, and so on. The total energy remains the same. What changes is the form of the energy and how useful it is. This distinction between energy conservation and energy degradation is a central theme of thermodynamics, which we will explore in a later lecture.

Misconception

"Potential energy is a property of a single object."

Potential energy is a property of a system of interacting objects. A book does not have gravitational potential energy by itself; the Earth-book system does. The energy is stored in the gravitational field between them. This is why moving the book changes the PE: you are changing the configuration of the system. In practice, because the Earth is so massive that it barely moves, we often speak loosely of "the book's potential energy," but strictly, it belongs to the system.

Misconception

"If kinetic energy is conserved, so is velocity."

No. Kinetic energy is a scalar (½mv²), while velocity is a vector. An object can change direction without changing speed, keeping its KE constant while its velocity (a vector) changes. Conversely, in some collisions, kinetic energy is conserved (elastic collisions) but individual velocities change dramatically. Conservation of KE and conservation of velocity (or momentum) are fundamentally different statements.

Key Takeaways

  • Work is defined as W = Fd cos θ. The angle between the force and displacement determines whether the work is positive (energy added), negative (energy removed), or zero (no energy transfer).
  • Kinetic energy, KE = ½mv², depends on the square of the speed. Doubling the speed quadruples the kinetic energy, which is why high-speed impacts are so much more destructive than low-speed ones.
  • The work-energy theorem states that the net work done on an object equals its change in kinetic energy: Wnet = ΔKE. This is a scalar equation that bypasses the need for vector decomposition.
  • Gravitational potential energy near Earth's surface is PE = mgh. The reference level for h is arbitrary; only changes in PE are physically meaningful.
  • Elastic potential energy is PE = ½kx² for an ideal spring, where x is the displacement from equilibrium.
  • In the absence of non-conservative forces, total mechanical energy is conserved: KE1 + PE1 = KE2 + PE2. This powerful principle connects initial and final states without requiring knowledge of the path.
  • Conservative forces (gravity, springs) do path-independent work and have associated potential energies. Non-conservative forces (friction, air resistance) do path-dependent work and convert mechanical energy into thermal or other forms.
  • Power is the rate of doing work: P = W/t = Fv. It tells us how quickly energy is being transferred. The SI unit is the watt (1 W = 1 J/s).
  • Energy is never created or destroyed—only transformed from one form to another. This law of conservation of energy is one of the most fundamental principles in all of physics, arising from the time-invariance of physical laws.
