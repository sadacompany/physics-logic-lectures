Overview

In the previous lectures we built the language of physics: units, dimensions, vectors, and the kinematics of motion. We learned how to describe motion—position, velocity, acceleration—but we never asked the deeper question: why do objects move the way they do? Why does a ball thrown upward slow down, stop, and reverse? Why does a car skid when the brakes are applied too hard? Why does the Moon orbit the Earth instead of flying off in a straight line? The answers to all these questions come from a single, breathtaking framework laid down by Isaac Newton in 1687: the three laws of motion.

Newton's laws are not merely historical curiosities. They are the foundation upon which virtually all of classical mechanics is built. Every bridge you cross, every aircraft that flies, every satellite that orbits, every car that brakes—all are governed by these three laws. Engineers use them daily. Physicists extend them to new domains. And students who truly understand them (rather than merely memorize them) gain an almost magical ability to reason about the physical world. This lecture is devoted to building that understanding from the ground up.

We will begin with the concept of force itself—what it is, and what it is not. We will then walk through each of Newton's three laws, not as statements to be memorized but as profound insights into how nature operates. Along the way, we will introduce the essential tools of mechanics: free body diagrams, weight, normal force, friction, and tension. By the end of this lecture, you will be able to analyze the forces acting on any object in a wide variety of physical situations, and predict its motion using Newton's laws.

The Concept of Force

Before we can state Newton's laws, we need to be precise about what we mean by force. In everyday language, force is synonymous with strength, power, or effort. In physics, force has a much more specific meaning, and getting this meaning right is essential to everything that follows.

Definition

A force is an interaction between two objects that can cause one or both of them to accelerate. Every force involves a push or a pull exerted by one object on another. Force is a vector quantity: it has both magnitude and direction. The SI unit of force is the newton (N), where 1 N = 1 kg · m/s².

The most important word in that definition is interaction. A force is not something that an object "has" or "possesses." A force is something that happens between two objects. When you push a wall, the force is an interaction between your hand and the wall. When the Earth pulls an apple downward, the gravitational force is an interaction between the Earth and the apple. There is no such thing as a force that involves only one object. This may seem like a pedantic point, but it is actually one of the deepest insights in mechanics and will become critically important when we discuss Newton's third law.

Forces can be broadly classified into two categories. Contact forces arise when objects are physically touching: you push a box, a rope pulls a sled, the ground supports your weight, friction resists sliding. Field forces (also called non-contact forces or action-at-a-distance forces) act without physical contact: gravity pulls you toward the Earth even though you are not "touching" the center of the Earth; a magnet attracts a paperclip across a gap of air; electric charges push and pull each other through empty space. In this lecture, we will focus primarily on contact forces and gravity, but the laws we develop apply to all forces without exception.

Because force is a vector, it is not enough to say "a 10-newton force acts on the object." You must also specify the direction: 10 newtons to the right, 10 newtons downward, 10 newtons at 30 degrees above the horizontal. When multiple forces act on the same object, we add them as vectors to find the net force (also called the resultant force or total force). It is the net force—not any individual force—that determines how the object accelerates. This principle is so important that it deserves to be called out explicitly: the motion of an object is determined entirely by the net force acting on it.

Newton's First Law: The Law of Inertia

The Aristotelian View and Its Downfall

For nearly two thousand years, the dominant theory of motion was that of Aristotle: an object moves only when a force acts on it, and the object stops when the force is removed. This seems almost self-evidently true from everyday experience. Push a book across a table, and it slides. Stop pushing, and the book stops. It looks as though the natural state of an object is to be at rest, and that force is needed to maintain motion.

But Galileo saw through this illusion. His great insight was to recognize that the book stops not because of the absence of force, but because of the presence of another force: friction. The table's rough surface exerts a frictional force on the book that opposes its motion and brings it to a halt. What if we could eliminate friction? Galileo conducted a brilliant thought experiment. Imagine rolling a ball down one inclined plane and up another. The ball rises almost to its original height on the second plane. Now reduce the angle of the second plane. The ball travels farther horizontally before reaching the same height. Make the second plane even shallower, and the ball travels farther still. What if the second plane is perfectly horizontal? The ball would never reach its original height, so it would roll forever—never stopping, never slowing, continuing in a straight line indefinitely.

This is a revolutionary idea. It says that the natural state of motion is not rest but constant velocity. An object in motion does not need a force to keep it moving. It needs a force only to change its motion—to speed it up, slow it down, or change its direction. Newton elevated this insight into the first of his three laws.

Newton's First Law

An object at rest remains at rest, and an object in motion continues in motion with constant velocity (constant speed in a straight line), unless acted upon by a net external force.

Inertia: The Resistance to Change

The first law introduces a crucial concept: inertia. Inertia is the tendency of an object to resist changes in its state of motion. An object at rest tends to stay at rest. An object moving in a straight line at constant speed tends to keep doing so. The more massive an object, the greater its inertia—it is harder to start it moving, harder to stop it, harder to change its direction. A loaded freight train has enormous inertia: it takes kilometers to stop even with brakes fully applied. A tennis ball has very little inertia: you can start it, stop it, and redirect it with a flick of your wrist.

Mass is the quantitative measure of inertia. This is, in fact, one way to define mass: mass is the property of an object that determines how much it resists acceleration when a force is applied. A 10-kilogram object has ten times the inertia of a 1-kilogram object, meaning it requires ten times the force to produce the same acceleration.

Condition for Equilibrium (First Law) ΣF = 0  ⇒  a = 0  (constant velocity or rest)

This formula is more than a mathematical statement. It tells us something profound: if the net force on an object is zero, the object does not accelerate. It either remains at rest or moves at constant velocity. Conversely, if an object is observed to be at rest or moving at constant velocity, we can conclude that the net force on it must be zero. This gives us a powerful diagnostic tool. Whenever you see an object that is not accelerating, you know—with certainty—that all the forces on it balance out perfectly.

Inertial Reference Frames

There is a subtle but important caveat to Newton's first law: it is only valid in an inertial reference frame—a frame of reference that is itself not accelerating. If you are sitting in a car that suddenly brakes, a coffee cup on the dashboard slides forward. From your perspective inside the decelerating car, it looks as though a force pushed the cup forward. But no such force exists. The cup simply continued moving at its original velocity (inertia!) while the car decelerated beneath it. The "mysterious force" is an artifact of observing from a non-inertial (accelerating) reference frame. Newton's laws, in their standard form, apply only in inertial frames—frames that are at rest or moving at constant velocity relative to distant stars.

Newton's Second Law: The Law of Acceleration

The Heart of Classical Mechanics

Newton's first law tells us what happens when the net force is zero: no acceleration. Newton's second law tells us what happens when the net force is not zero: the object accelerates. More precisely, it tells us exactly how much the object accelerates and in what direction. This is the quantitative powerhouse of mechanics—the equation that allows us to predict the motion of everything from falling apples to orbiting planets.

Newton's Second Law ΣF = ma

The boldface reminds us that this is a vector equation: the net force and the acceleration point in the same direction. In component form, this single vector equation becomes two (in 2D) or three (in 3D) scalar equations:

Component Form (2D) ΣFx = max      ΣFy = may

What F = ma Really Means

Let us unpack this equation carefully, because its simplicity conceals enormous depth.

ΣF is the net force—the vector sum of all forces acting on the object. Not the strongest force, not the "applied" force, not any single force in isolation, but the total, combined effect of every force acting on the object. If three forces act on a box (a push to the right, gravity downward, and the floor pushing upward), you must add all three as vectors to find ΣF. Only the net force matters for determining the acceleration.

m is the mass of the object—a scalar quantity that measures how much the object resists acceleration. Mass is always positive and does not depend on location. Your mass is the same on the Moon as on Earth, even though your weight is different. Mass is the bridge between force and acceleration: it tells you how much acceleration a given force will produce.

a is the acceleration of the object—the rate at which its velocity changes. The equation says that the acceleration is directly proportional to the net force and inversely proportional to the mass. Double the force, double the acceleration. Double the mass, halve the acceleration. The acceleration always points in the same direction as the net force. This is perhaps the single most important sentence in classical mechanics: the acceleration of an object is always in the direction of the net force acting on it.

Notice what the second law does not say. It does not say that force causes velocity. It says that force causes acceleration—a change in velocity. An object can be moving to the right while the net force (and therefore the acceleration) points to the left. This happens all the time: a ball thrown upward is moving upward, but gravity (the net force) pulls it downward, causing it to decelerate. The force and the velocity do not have to point in the same direction. But the force and the acceleration always do.

Worked Example: Pushing a Cart

Problem: A 25-kg shopping cart is pushed with a horizontal force of 60 N across a level floor. The frictional force opposing the motion is 10 N. Find the acceleration of the cart.

Solution

Step 1: Identify forces in the horizontal direction.
Applied force: Fapp = 60 N (forward)
Friction: f = 10 N (backward)

Step 2: Find the net horizontal force.
ΣFx = 60 N − 10 N = 50 N (forward)

Step 3: Apply Newton's second law.
ΣFx = max
50 N = (25 kg)(ax)
ax = 50 / 25 = 2.0 m/s² (forward)

Check: The units work out (N / kg = m/s²). The magnitude is reasonable: a moderate push on a light cart produces a gentle acceleration, about one-fifth of g. The direction is forward, which makes sense since the push exceeds the friction.

The First Law as a Special Case

Notice that Newton's first law is actually a special case of the second law. If ΣF = 0, then by the second law, ma = 0, which means a = 0 (since m ≠ 0). Zero acceleration means constant velocity—which is precisely what the first law says. So why state the first law separately? Because the first law does something the second law alone does not: it establishes the existence of inertial reference frames. It says, in effect, "there exist frames of reference in which an object with no net force on it does not accelerate." This is a foundational statement about the structure of space and time, not merely a special case of F = ma.

Newton's Third Law: Action and Reaction

Forces Come in Pairs

Newton's third law is arguably the most misunderstood of the three, and yet it is astonishingly simple once grasped correctly. It states:

Newton's Third Law

If object A exerts a force on object B, then object B simultaneously exerts a force on object A that is equal in magnitude and opposite in direction. These two forces act on different objects.

In symbolic form: FA on B = −FB on A. The forces are equal in magnitude, opposite in direction, of the same type (both gravitational, both contact, both electromagnetic), and they act on different objects. This last point is the one students most often miss, and it is the source of nearly all confusion about the third law.

When you push a wall with 50 N of force, the wall pushes back on you with 50 N. When the Earth pulls you downward with gravitational force (your weight), you pull the Earth upward with exactly the same force. When a hammer strikes a nail, the nail exerts just as much force on the hammer as the hammer exerts on the nail. These are not separate, independent phenomena. They are the same interaction viewed from two perspectives. A force is an interaction between two objects, and every interaction is inherently two-sided.

The Horse-and-Cart Paradox

A classic puzzle that trips up students goes like this: "If the horse pulls the cart forward with a force of 500 N, and the cart pulls the horse backward with a force of 500 N (by Newton's third law), how does the system ever move? Don't the forces cancel out?"

The resolution is beautiful in its simplicity: the two forces act on different objects. The horse pulls the cart forward—this force acts on the cart. The cart pulls the horse backward—this force acts on the horse. You cannot add forces that act on different objects. To determine whether the cart accelerates, you look only at the forces acting on the cart: the forward pull from the horse, minus the backward friction from the ground on the cart's wheels. To determine whether the horse accelerates, you look only at the forces acting on the horse: the forward friction from the ground on the horse's hooves, minus the backward pull from the cart.

The horse moves forward because the friction between its hooves and the ground (pushing the horse forward) exceeds the backward pull of the cart. The cart moves forward because the horse's pull (forward on the cart) exceeds the friction on the cart's wheels. The system accelerates forward because, when you analyze each object separately, the net force on each object points forward. The third-law pairs never cancel because they never act on the same object.

Key Concept

Third-law force pairs never cancel each other because they act on different objects. Forces can only cancel when they act on the same object. When analyzing the motion of any object, consider only the forces acting on that object, never the forces it exerts on other objects.

Identifying Third-Law Pairs

A reliable method for identifying third-law pairs is the "A-on-B / B-on-A" test. For every force you identify, ask: "What exerts this force, and what is it exerted on?" Then swap the roles. For example: gravity on a book is "Earth pulls book downward." The third-law partner is "Book pulls Earth upward." The normal force of the table on the book is "Table pushes book upward." The partner is "Book pushes table downward." Notice that the normal force on the book and the weight of the book are not a third-law pair, even though they are equal and opposite when the book is in equilibrium. They are both forces on the same object (the book) and happen to balance; they are not the same interaction viewed from two sides.

Free Body Diagrams

The Physicist's Most Important Sketch

A free body diagram (FBD) is a simplified drawing that shows a single object isolated from its environment, with arrows representing every force acting on that object. It is the single most important tool in all of Newtonian mechanics. Attempting to solve a force problem without drawing a free body diagram is like attempting to navigate a city without a map—you might get lucky occasionally, but you will be lost more often than not.

Step-by-Step Method

Drawing a free body diagram is a systematic process. Follow these steps every time, without exception:

  1. Isolate the object. Choose the specific object whose motion you want to analyze. Draw it as a simple shape (a dot or a box). Remove everything else from the picture—the ground, the ropes, the inclined plane, the other objects. Your diagram shows only the object of interest.
  2. Identify all forces acting on the object. Systematically consider: Is there gravity? (Almost always yes.) Is there a surface in contact? (If so, there is a normal force perpendicular to the surface and possibly friction parallel to it.) Is there a rope or string? (If so, there is tension along the rope.) Is there an applied push or pull? Is there air resistance? Go through each possibility deliberately.
  3. Draw each force as an arrow starting from the object. The arrow's direction shows the force's direction. Its length should roughly represent the force's magnitude (longer arrows for stronger forces). Label each arrow with a symbol: W or mg for weight, N for normal force, f for friction, T for tension, Fapp for an applied force.
  4. Choose a coordinate system. For problems on flat ground, use horizontal and vertical axes. For inclined planes, tilt your axes so that one axis runs along the surface and the other runs perpendicular to it. This choice of coordinates simplifies the math enormously.
  5. Resolve forces into components. Decompose any force that does not lie along one of your coordinate axes into its components. Apply Newton's second law separately in each direction: ΣFx = max and ΣFy = may.

Example: Block on an Inclined Plane

Consider a block of mass m resting on a frictionless inclined plane that makes an angle θ with the horizontal. What forces act on the block, and what is its acceleration?

Worked Example: Inclined Plane (No Friction)

Step 1: Isolate the block. Draw it as a point or a small box on the incline.

Step 2: Identify forces. Gravity pulls the block straight down with magnitude mg. The surface pushes the block perpendicular to the incline with normal force N. There is no friction (the problem says frictionless), no rope, no applied force. Two forces total.

Step 3: Choose coordinates. Tilt the axes: let the x-axis point down the incline (the direction of expected motion) and the y-axis point perpendicular to the surface (away from it).

Step 4: Resolve gravity into components along the tilted axes.
Component along the incline (x): mg sin θ (pointing down the slope)
Component perpendicular to incline (y): mg cos θ (pointing into the surface)

Step 5: Apply Newton's second law in each direction.

Solution

Perpendicular to incline (y-direction): The block does not accelerate perpendicular to the surface, so ay = 0.
ΣFy = N − mg cos θ = 0
Therefore: N = mg cos θ

Along the incline (x-direction):
ΣFx = mg sin θ = max
Therefore: a = g sin θ

Check: When θ = 0 (flat surface), a = g sin 0 = 0. The block does not accelerate on a flat surface. When θ = 90° (vertical drop), a = g sin 90° = g. The block is in free fall. Both limiting cases make perfect physical sense, which gives us confidence the answer is correct.

Weight and Normal Force

Weight: The Force of Gravity

Weight is the gravitational force exerted on an object by a massive body (usually the Earth). It is not the same as mass. Mass is an intrinsic property of the object—a measure of how much matter it contains and how much it resists acceleration. Weight is a force that depends on where the object is located. On the surface of the Earth, weight and mass are related by:

Weight W = mg

where m is the mass of the object and g is the acceleration due to gravity (approximately 9.8 m/s² near Earth's surface). On the Moon, where g is about 1.6 m/s², a 70-kg astronaut weighs only 112 N, compared to 686 N on Earth. The astronaut's mass is the same in both places; the weight changes because g changes. Weight always points toward the center of the gravitational body—straight down, for objects near Earth's surface.

Normal Force: The Surface Pushes Back

When an object rests on a surface, the surface exerts a force on the object that prevents it from falling through. This is the normal force, and it is always perpendicular ("normal" in the mathematical sense) to the surface. On a flat table, the normal force points straight up. On an inclined plane, it points perpendicular to the slope. On a wall, it points horizontally away from the wall.

A critical point: the normal force is not always equal to the weight. It adjusts itself to whatever value is needed to prevent the object from accelerating through the surface. On a flat surface with no other vertical forces, N = mg. But if you push down on the object, the normal force increases. If you pull up on the object (but not enough to lift it), the normal force decreases. The normal force is a responsive force—it responds to the situation rather than having a fixed predetermined value.

The Elevator Problem

The elevator is the classic scenario for understanding the difference between weight and normal force, and for experiencing what it means for the normal force to change.

Worked Example: Person in an Elevator

Problem: A 70-kg person stands on a bathroom scale in an elevator. The scale reads the normal force. What does the scale read when the elevator (a) is at rest, (b) accelerates upward at 2 m/s², (c) accelerates downward at 2 m/s², and (d) is in free fall?

Solution

The person's weight is W = mg = (70)(9.8) = 686 N in all cases. Apply Newton's second law in the vertical direction, taking upward as positive.

General equation: N − mg = ma  ⇒  N = m(g + a)

(a) At rest (a = 0):
N = 70(9.8 + 0) = 686 N
The scale reads the true weight. You feel "normal."

(b) Accelerating upward (a = +2 m/s²):
N = 70(9.8 + 2) = 70(11.8) = 826 N
The scale reads more than your weight. You feel heavier.

(c) Accelerating downward (a = −2 m/s²):
N = 70(9.8 − 2) = 70(7.8) = 546 N
The scale reads less than your weight. You feel lighter.

(d) Free fall (a = −9.8 m/s²):
N = 70(9.8 − 9.8) = 0 N
The scale reads zero. You are weightless—not because gravity has disappeared, but because everything (you, the scale, the elevator) is accelerating downward at the same rate. There is no contact force between you and the scale.

This example illustrates a profound point: what we feel as weight is not the gravitational force itself, but the normal force (or, more generally, the contact force from whatever supports us). Astronauts on the International Space Station are not "beyond gravity"—gravity at that altitude is about 90% of its surface value. They feel weightless because they and the station are in free fall together, orbiting the Earth, and there is no contact force between them and the station floor.

Friction

The Force That Opposes Sliding

Friction is a contact force that opposes the relative motion (or attempted motion) between two surfaces. It arises from the microscopic roughness of surfaces—tiny peaks and valleys on each surface interlock and resist sliding. Even surfaces that appear perfectly smooth under the naked eye are rough at the microscopic scale.

There are two types of friction that behave differently: static friction and kinetic friction.

Static Friction

Static friction acts when two surfaces are in contact but not sliding relative to each other. You push a heavy crate with a small force, and it does not move. Static friction is matching your applied force, keeping the net force zero. Push harder, and static friction increases to match. It is a responsive force, just like the normal force—it adjusts to whatever value is needed to prevent sliding, up to a maximum limit.

Static Friction fs ≤ μs N

The symbol μs is the coefficient of static friction, a dimensionless number that depends on the nature of the two surfaces in contact. The inequality is critical: static friction can be anywhere from zero (when no horizontal force is applied) up to the maximum value μsN (the threshold of sliding). Once the applied force exceeds μsN, static friction can no longer hold, and the object begins to slide.

Kinetic Friction

Kinetic friction acts when two surfaces are already sliding relative to each other. Once the crate starts moving, the frictional force drops slightly and becomes approximately constant:

Kinetic Friction fk = μk N

The coefficient of kinetic friction μk is typically less than μs for the same pair of surfaces. This is why it is harder to start an object sliding than to keep it sliding. Once you overcome the static friction threshold and the object begins to move, less force is needed to maintain the motion.

Key Properties of Friction

Several features of the friction model deserve emphasis:

  • Friction is proportional to the normal force, not to the surface area of contact. A brick lying flat and a brick standing on its end experience the same frictional force (assuming the same normal force and same surfaces), even though the contact areas are different. This is counterintuitive but experimentally verified.
  • Kinetic friction is approximately independent of speed. Whether the crate slides at 1 m/s or 5 m/s, the kinetic friction is roughly the same. (At very high speeds, this approximation breaks down.)
  • Friction always opposes the relative motion (or tendency of motion) between surfaces. If a block slides to the right, friction points to the left. If the block would slide downhill without friction, static friction points uphill.
Worked Example: Pulling a Sled

Problem: A child pulls a 15-kg sled across flat snow with a horizontal force of 40 N. The coefficient of kinetic friction between the sled runners and the snow is μk = 0.10. Find the acceleration of the sled.

Solution

Free body diagram (vertical): Weight W = mg = (15)(9.8) = 147 N downward. Normal force N upward. No vertical acceleration, so N = W = 147 N.

Kinetic friction: fk = μkN = (0.10)(147) = 14.7 N, directed backward (opposing motion).

Net horizontal force:
ΣFx = 40 N − 14.7 N = 25.3 N (forward)

Acceleration:
a = ΣFx / m = 25.3 / 15 = 1.69 m/s²

Check: The acceleration is positive (forward), which makes sense because the pull exceeds the friction. The magnitude is modest—about 17% of g—which seems reasonable for a child pulling a sled on snow.

Tension

The Force in a Rope

Tension is the force transmitted through a rope, string, cable, or chain when it is pulled taut by forces acting from opposite ends. When you pull a dog on a leash, you exert a force on the leash, and the leash transmits that force to the dog's collar. The leash is under tension, and the tension force pulls on whatever is attached to each end.

In introductory physics, we almost always make two simplifying assumptions about ropes:

  • Massless rope: The rope has negligible mass compared to the objects it connects. This means that the tension is the same at every point along the rope. Why? Because if a rope segment has zero mass, Newton's second law (F = ma = 0 · a = 0) requires that the net force on it is zero, which means the tension pulling it from the left must equal the tension pulling it from the right. The tension is therefore uniform throughout the rope.
  • Inextensible rope: The rope does not stretch. This means that the objects at each end have the same magnitude of acceleration (if they are connected by a single rope over a frictionless pulley).

Tension always pulls; it never pushes. A rope can pull an object toward it, but it cannot push an object away. This is because a rope, unlike a rigid rod, collapses when you try to push on it. The direction of the tension force is always along the rope, away from the object in question.

Pulleys and the Atwood Machine

A pulley is a device that redirects the direction of a tension force. An ideal (frictionless, massless) pulley changes the direction of the rope without changing the magnitude of the tension. This is extremely useful in practice: it allows you to lift a heavy object by pulling downward on a rope instead of pushing upward.

The Atwood machine is a classic physics apparatus: two masses connected by a rope that passes over a single frictionless pulley. It is one of the most elegant and instructive systems in mechanics.

Worked Example: The Atwood Machine

Problem: Two masses, m1 = 5 kg and m2 = 3 kg, are connected by a massless rope over a frictionless pulley. Find the acceleration of the system and the tension in the rope.

Solution

Key insight: Because the rope is inextensible, both masses have the same magnitude of acceleration a. The heavier mass accelerates downward and the lighter mass accelerates upward.

FBD for m1 (heavier, accelerates down):
m1g − T = m1a   … (1)

FBD for m2 (lighter, accelerates up):
T − m2g = m2a   … (2)

Add equations (1) and (2):
m1g − m2g = (m1 + m2)a
a = (m1 − m2)g / (m1 + m2)

a = (5 − 3)(9.8) / (5 + 3) = (2)(9.8) / 8 = 2.45 m/s²

Find tension from equation (2):
T = m2(g + a) = 3(9.8 + 2.45) = 3(12.25) = 36.75 N

Check: Is T between m2g = 29.4 N and m1g = 49.0 N? Yes: 29.4 < 36.75 < 49.0. This makes physical sense—the tension must exceed the weight of the lighter mass (to accelerate it upward) and must be less than the weight of the heavier mass (to allow it to accelerate downward). If m1 = m2, then a = 0 and T = mg, which is the equilibrium case. If m2 = 0, then a = g and T = 0, which is free fall. Both limiting cases check out.

Applications: Connected Objects

Many real-world problems involve multiple objects connected by ropes, in contact, or stacked. The strategy is always the same: draw a free body diagram for each object, write Newton's second law for each object, and solve the resulting system of equations. The constraint provided by the connection (same acceleration if connected by an inextensible rope, same acceleration if in contact) gives you the additional equation needed to solve for all unknowns.

Worked Example: Two Blocks Connected on a Table

Problem: A block of mass m1 = 4 kg sits on a frictionless table. It is connected by a massless rope to a block of mass m2 = 6 kg that hangs over the edge of the table via a frictionless pulley. Find the acceleration and the tension in the rope.

Solution

Both blocks have the same magnitude of acceleration a. Block 1 accelerates horizontally (toward the pulley). Block 2 accelerates downward.

FBD for m1 (on the table, horizontal motion):
T = m1a   … (1)

FBD for m2 (hanging, vertical motion):
m2g − T = m2a   … (2)

Add equations (1) and (2):
m2g = (m1 + m2)a
a = m2g / (m1 + m2) = (6)(9.8) / (4 + 6) = 58.8 / 10 = 5.88 m/s²

Tension from equation (1):
T = m1a = (4)(5.88) = 23.5 N

Check: The acceleration is less than g (the hanging block cannot fall faster than free fall). The tension (23.5 N) is less than m2g = 58.8 N, which is correct because the hanging block is accelerating downward (net downward force is positive). If m1 = 0, then a = g and T = 0 (pure free fall). If m1 is very large, a approaches 0 and T approaches m2g (the heavy block on the table barely moves, and the rope simply supports the hanging block). Both limits are physically reasonable.

The method demonstrated here—separate FBDs, separate equations, solve the system—scales to any number of connected objects. Whether you have two blocks, three blocks, a system of pulleys, or a chain of objects on surfaces with friction, the approach is identical. The only thing that changes is the number of equations you need to write and solve.

Worked Example: Block on Incline with Hanging Mass

Problem: A 3-kg block sits on a frictionless incline at 30°. A massless rope connects it over a pulley at the top of the incline to a 2-kg block that hangs vertically. Which way does the system move, and what is the acceleration?

Solution

First, determine which direction the system tends to move. The component of gravity pulling the 3-kg block down the incline is m1g sin 30° = (3)(9.8)(0.5) = 14.7 N. The weight of the hanging block is m2g = (2)(9.8) = 19.6 N. Since 19.6 N > 14.7 N, the hanging block falls and the block on the incline moves up the slope.

FBD for m1 (on incline, along the slope, up-the-incline positive):
T − m1g sin θ = m1a   … (1)

FBD for m2 (hanging, downward positive):
m2g − T = m2a   … (2)

Add:
m2g − m1g sin θ = (m1 + m2)a
a = (m2g − m1g sin θ) / (m1 + m2)
a = (19.6 − 14.7) / 5 = 4.9 / 5 = 0.98 m/s²

Common Misconceptions

Newton's laws seem straightforward when stated in textbook language, but students consistently fall into the same conceptual traps. Confronting these misconceptions head-on is essential for building genuine understanding.

Misconception

"An object in motion must have a net force acting on it." — This is the Aristotelian view, and it is wrong. Newton's first law explicitly states that an object can move at constant velocity with zero net force. A hockey puck sliding on frictionless ice continues moving in a straight line at constant speed with no force needed to maintain that motion. A force is needed only to change the velocity. If an object is moving at constant velocity, the net force on it is exactly zero. Motion does not require force; changes in motion require force.

Misconception

"Heavier objects fall faster." — In a vacuum (no air resistance), all objects fall with the same acceleration g, regardless of mass. A hammer and a feather dropped on the Moon (where there is no air) hit the ground at the same time—as demonstrated by Apollo 15 astronaut David Scott. On Earth, air resistance complicates matters and can make lighter objects fall more slowly, but this is the effect of air resistance, not a failure of Newton's laws. The gravitational force on a heavier object is greater, but so is its mass, and the two effects cancel exactly: a = F/m = mg/m = g.

Misconception

"The normal force always equals the weight." — The normal force equals the weight only in the special case of an object on a horizontal surface with no other vertical forces. If the surface is inclined, N = mg cos θ, which is less than mg. If someone pushes down on the object, N exceeds mg. If someone pulls up on it, N is less than mg. If the object is on a vertical wall, the normal force is horizontal and has nothing to do with the weight at all. The normal force is whatever value is needed to prevent the object from passing through the surface. Always calculate it from Newton's second law; never assume it equals mg.

Misconception

"Action and reaction forces cancel out." — This is the single most common error in applying Newton's third law. Third-law force pairs never cancel because they act on different objects. The force of the Earth on you (your weight) and the force of you on the Earth are a third-law pair. They do not cancel because one acts on you and the other acts on the Earth. Forces can only cancel when they act on the same object. The weight pulling you down and the normal force of the floor pushing you up act on the same object (you) and can cancel—but they are not a third-law pair. They are two different forces from two different interactions that happen to be equal and opposite when you are in equilibrium.

Misconception

"Force is needed to keep an object moving in a circle at constant speed." — This one is actually true, and the misconception is thinking it contradicts the first law. An object moving in a circle at constant speed is still accelerating, because its direction is constantly changing. The velocity vector changes direction even though its magnitude stays the same. This change in direction requires a centripetal (center-seeking) force. Without that force, the object would fly off in a straight line—exactly as the first law predicts. Circular motion does not violate the first law; it illustrates it.

Key Takeaways

Summary

  • A force is an interaction between two objects—a push or a pull. It is a vector quantity measured in newtons (N). The net force on an object is the vector sum of all individual forces acting on it.
  • Newton's first law (inertia): An object remains at rest or moves at constant velocity unless a net external force acts on it. The natural state of motion is constant velocity, not rest. Force is needed to change motion, not to maintain it.
  • Newton's second law: ΣF = ma. The net force on an object equals its mass times its acceleration. Acceleration is always in the direction of the net force. This is the quantitative engine of mechanics.
  • Newton's third law: Forces always come in pairs. If A pushes B, then B pushes A with equal magnitude in the opposite direction. These paired forces act on different objects and therefore never cancel each other.
  • Free body diagrams are essential. Isolate the object, identify all forces, draw arrows, choose a coordinate system, resolve into components, and apply ΣF = ma in each direction. Never skip this step.
  • Weight (W = mg) is the gravitational force on an object, always directed toward the center of the Earth. The normal force is the perpendicular contact force from a surface, and it is not always equal to the weight.
  • Friction opposes relative motion between surfaces. Static friction (fs ≤ μsN) prevents sliding and adjusts up to a maximum. Kinetic friction (fk = μkN) acts during sliding and is typically less than the maximum static friction.
  • Tension is the pulling force transmitted through a rope or string. In an ideal (massless, inextensible) rope, tension is the same at every point. Pulleys redirect the tension without changing its magnitude.
  • For connected objects, draw a separate FBD for each object, write Newton's second law for each, and use the constraint (same acceleration for objects connected by an inextensible rope) to solve the system of equations.
  • The most common misconceptions involve confusing force with velocity, assuming the normal force always equals weight, and believing that third-law pairs cancel. Recognizing and correcting these errors is essential to mastering mechanics.
