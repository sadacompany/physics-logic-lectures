Learning Goals

  • Deeply understand all three of Newton's laws and the physical reasoning behind each
  • Draw correct free body diagrams for objects in a variety of physical situations

Overview

Isaac Newton published his Philosophiae Naturalis Principia Mathematica in 1687, and in doing so laid down the framework that would dominate physics for over two centuries. At the heart of the Principia are three deceptively simple laws that, together, explain why objects move the way they do. These laws do not merely describe motion—that was the work of kinematics, which we covered in earlier lectures. Instead, Newton's laws answer the deeper question: what causes motion to change?

The answer, in a word, is force. But "force" in physics is far more precise than the everyday word suggests. In this lecture we will build a rigorous understanding of force, then use it to state and explore each of Newton's three laws. Along the way, we will develop the indispensable technique of free body diagrams, examine the most common forces encountered in introductory mechanics—weight, normal force, friction, and tension—and work through applications that tie everything together.

By the end of this lecture, you will possess the conceptual and mathematical tools to analyze the forces acting on any object and predict how that object will accelerate. This is the foundation upon which all of classical mechanics rests, and it is essential preparation for the energy methods and momentum principles we will encounter in the lectures that follow.

The Concept of Force

Before we can state Newton's laws, we need a clear understanding of what a force actually is. In everyday language, we use the word loosely—the "force" of an argument, the "force" of habit. In physics, force has a precise meaning: it is an interaction between two objects that can cause one or both of them to accelerate. Notice the word interaction. A force is never something that belongs to a single object in isolation; it is always something that happens between two objects.

Definition: Force

A force is a push or pull exerted on an object as a result of its interaction with another object. Forces are vector quantities—they have both magnitude and direction. The SI unit of force is the newton (N), where 1 N = 1 kg · m/s².

Forces can be broadly classified into two categories. Contact forces arise when objects physically touch: the push of your hand against a wall, the friction between a tire and the road, the tension in a rope. Field forces (also called action-at-a-distance forces) act without physical contact: gravity pulls a ball toward the Earth even through empty space, and electric charges attract or repel each other across a vacuum.

It is critical to understand that a force does not "live inside" an object. A book resting on a table does not "contain" a gravitational force. Rather, there is a gravitational interaction between the book and the Earth, and as a result the Earth pulls the book downward and the book pulls the Earth upward. Similarly, the table pushes up on the book and the book pushes down on the table. Every force you will ever encounter comes in a pair of mutual interactions—a fact that Newton elevated to the status of a law, as we shall see.

When multiple forces act on a single object, we can add them as vectors to find the net force (or resultant force), often written as ΣF. The net force is what determines the object's acceleration. An object might have a dozen forces acting on it, but what matters for its motion is their vector sum. If the net force is zero, the forces are said to be balanced, and the object does not accelerate. If the net force is nonzero, the forces are unbalanced, and the object accelerates in the direction of the net force.

Newton's First Law

Newton's First Law is sometimes called the Law of Inertia, and its intellectual roots stretch back well before Newton himself. The ancient Greek philosopher Aristotle believed that the natural state of an object was to be at rest, and that a force was needed to keep anything moving. This seems intuitively reasonable—after all, if you stop pushing a box across the floor, it stops. But Aristotle's view conflates two different things: the applied push and the friction that opposes it.

It was Galileo Galilei who first challenged this view with a brilliant thought experiment. Imagine rolling a ball down one inclined plane and up another. If the second plane is steep, the ball rises to nearly its original height but doesn't travel far horizontally. If the second plane is more gradual, the ball travels farther horizontally before reaching the same height. Now, what if the second plane is perfectly horizontal? Galileo reasoned that the ball would roll forever, because it would never reach its original height—there would be no reason for it to stop. In the idealized case of zero friction, an object in motion would remain in motion indefinitely.

Newton generalized Galileo's insight into a precise law. In modern language:

Newton's First Law (Law of Inertia)

An object at rest remains at rest, and an object in motion continues to move with constant velocity (constant speed in a straight line), unless acted upon by a net external force.

The law makes two related claims. First, an object at rest will stay at rest if no net force acts on it—this part is intuitive. Second, and more profoundly, an object already moving will continue moving in a straight line at the same speed forever if no net force acts on it. It is this second claim that overturns Aristotle. Motion itself does not require a force; changes in motion require a force.

The property of matter that resists changes in velocity is called inertia. An object with more mass has more inertia and is harder to accelerate (or decelerate). A loaded freight train has enormous inertia—it takes a large force applied over a long time to bring it up to speed, and an equally large effort to bring it to a stop. A tennis ball, by contrast, has very little inertia and can be accelerated to high speeds with a quick swing of a racket.

First Law in Symbolic Form

If ΣF = 0, then a = 0 (and therefore v = constant)

When the vector sum of all forces on an object is zero, its acceleration is zero and its velocity does not change. This includes the special case where the velocity is zero (object at rest).

It is worth noting that the First Law also defines what physicists call an inertial reference frame—a frame of reference in which the law holds true. If you are riding in a car that suddenly brakes, a coffee cup on the dashboard slides forward even though no horizontal force acts on it. In the accelerating reference frame of the car, Newton's First Law appears to be violated. But from the perspective of an observer standing on the sidewalk (an inertial frame), the cup simply continues moving forward at constant velocity while the car decelerates beneath it. Newton's laws are valid only in inertial reference frames, and the First Law serves as the test for whether a given frame qualifies.

To summarize, the First Law tells us that force is not what sustains motion—force is what changes motion. This is a conceptual revolution that students must internalize before proceeding to the quantitative power of the Second Law.

Newton's Second Law

If the First Law tells us when an object accelerates (when the net force is nonzero), the Second Law tells us how much it accelerates and in what direction. It is the quantitative heart of Newtonian mechanics and the equation you will use more than any other in this course.

Newton's Second Law

The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass. The acceleration is in the same direction as the net force.

Newton's Second Law

ΣF = ma

Here ΣF is the net (total) force on the object, m is the object's mass, and a is its acceleration. This is a vector equation: it holds independently for each component (x, y, z).

Let us unpack this equation carefully. The left side, ΣF, is the vector sum of every force acting on the object. You must identify all forces—gravity, normal force, friction, tension, applied pushes, air resistance—and add them as vectors. Missing even one force will give a wrong answer.

On the right side, the mass m plays a crucial role. It is not merely a number that converts force into acceleration; it represents the object's resistance to acceleration. For the same net force, a more massive object accelerates less. This is the quantitative expression of inertia. Mass is a scalar (it has no direction), it is always positive, and in Newtonian mechanics it is constant—it does not change with speed or position.

The direction of the acceleration a is always the same as the direction of the net force ΣF. If you push a shopping cart to the right with a net force to the right, it accelerates to the right. If friction provides a net force to the left on a sliding block, the block decelerates (its acceleration is to the left, opposing its rightward velocity). Acceleration and net force are always parallel—never perpendicular, never at some random angle.

Because this is a vector equation, we almost always work with it in component form:

Component Form

ΣFx = max      ΣFy = may

Each component equation is independent. For many problems, choosing a clever coordinate system (e.g., tilted along an incline) makes one component of acceleration zero, simplifying the math.

Example: Pushing a Cart

A shopper pushes a 12 kg grocery cart with a horizontal force of 30 N. Friction exerts a force of 6 N opposing the motion. Find the cart's acceleration.

Solution:

Take the direction of the push as positive. The net force along the horizontal direction is:

ΣFx = 30 N − 6 N = 24 N

Applying Newton's Second Law:

ax = ΣFx / m = 24 N / 12 kg = 2.0 m/s²

The cart accelerates at 2.0 m/s² in the direction of the push. Notice that the acceleration is determined by the net force, not the applied force alone. If friction were larger—say 30 N to match the push—the net force would be zero and the cart would move at constant velocity (or remain at rest), consistent with the First Law.

A few common pitfalls deserve mention. First, F = ma applies to a single object. The mass is the mass of that one object, and the forces are only those acting on that object. Forces exerted by that object on other things do not appear in its own equation. Second, the Second Law is instantaneous: the acceleration at any moment equals the net force at that moment divided by the mass. If the force changes, the acceleration changes immediately. Third, if the acceleration is zero, the Second Law reduces to ΣF = 0, which is exactly the First Law. The First Law is therefore a special case of the Second.

Newton's Third Law

Newton's Third Law is perhaps the most frequently misunderstood of the three. Its statement is simple:

Newton's Third Law (Action-Reaction)

If object A exerts a force on object B, then object B exerts a force on object A that is equal in magnitude and opposite in direction. These two forces act on different objects.

The key phrase is "different objects." Every force in the universe comes in an action-reaction pair, but the two forces in the pair never act on the same object. When you push against a wall, the wall pushes back on you with equal force. When the Earth pulls you downward with gravity, you pull the Earth upward with the same gravitational force. The reason you accelerate toward the Earth (when you jump off a ledge) and the Earth does not noticeably accelerate toward you is not that the forces are different—they are exactly equal—but that the Earth's mass is incomprehensibly larger than yours, so its acceleration (by the Second Law) is immeasurably tiny.

Symbolically, if object A exerts a force FA→B on object B, then:

Third Law Pair

FA→B = −FB→A

The forces are equal in magnitude, opposite in direction, the same type of force (both gravitational, both contact, etc.), and they act on different objects.

A classic puzzle that tests your understanding of the Third Law is the horse-and-cart paradox. A horse pulls a cart. By the Third Law, the cart pulls back on the horse with an equal and opposite force. If the forces are equal and opposite, how does the system ever accelerate? The resolution lies in recognizing that the two forces act on different objects. To determine whether the horse accelerates, we examine the forces on the horse alone: the backward pull of the cart, and the forward friction of the ground on the horse's hooves. If the ground pushes the horse forward more than the cart pulls it back, the horse has a net forward force and accelerates forward. Similarly, the cart accelerates forward because the horse's pull exceeds the friction and air resistance on the cart. The Third Law pairs do not cancel because they act on different objects.

Why Third-Law Pairs Don't Cancel

Action-reaction forces act on different objects. When applying ΣF = ma, you consider only the forces on a single object. The reaction force is on the other object, so it does not enter into the first object's equation. Forces cancel only when two equal and opposite forces act on the same object.

To correctly identify Third Law pairs, always name both objects involved. "The table pushes up on the book" pairs with "the book pushes down on the table." "The Earth pulls the ball down" pairs with "the ball pulls the Earth up." If you cannot name two distinct objects, you have not identified a true Third Law pair. A common mistake is to say that the normal force on a book resting on a table is the reaction to gravity. It is not. The normal force on the book (exerted by the table) and the weight of the book (exerted by the Earth) are two different forces acting on the same object—they happen to be equal in this case, but that is a consequence of the First Law (equilibrium), not the Third Law.

Free Body Diagrams

A free body diagram (FBD) is the single most important tool for solving problems in Newtonian mechanics. It is a simplified drawing that shows a single object isolated from its surroundings, with arrows representing every force acting on that object. The "free" in the name means the object is freed from its environment—we strip away the table, the rope, the incline, and replace each contact with the appropriate force vector.

Drawing a correct free body diagram is both an art and a discipline. Here is a systematic procedure:

  1. Identify the object of interest. Decide which single object you are analyzing. Draw it as a simple shape (a dot or a box).
  2. Identify all forces acting on the object. Go through each type: Is gravity acting? Is the object in contact with a surface (normal force)? Is there friction? Is a rope or string attached (tension)? Is someone pushing or pulling (applied force)? Is there air resistance?
  3. Draw each force as an arrow originating from the object, pointing in the direction the force acts, with a label (W, N, f, T, Fapp, etc.). Make the arrow lengths roughly proportional to the magnitudes if known.
  4. Choose a coordinate system. For objects on flat surfaces, use horizontal and vertical axes. For inclined planes, tilt your axes so one axis is parallel to the incline and the other is perpendicular—this simplifies the math enormously.
  5. Do not include forces exerted by the object. Only forces acting on the object appear in its free body diagram. The reaction forces (Third Law pairs) belong in the free body diagrams of other objects.

Example: Block on an Inclined Plane

A 5.0 kg block sits on a frictionless incline tilted at 30° from the horizontal. Draw the free body diagram and find the block's acceleration down the incline.

Step 1: The object is the block. Draw it as a dot on the incline.

Step 2: Forces acting on the block:

  • Weight (W): magnitude mg = 5.0 × 9.8 = 49 N, directed straight down.
  • Normal force (N): perpendicular to the incline surface, directed away from the surface (up-left in this geometry).
  • No friction (frictionless surface), no tension, no applied force.

Step 3: Choose axes: x-axis along the incline (positive down the slope), y-axis perpendicular to the incline (positive away from the surface).

Step 4: Resolve weight into components:

Wx = mg sin 30° = 49 × 0.50 = 24.5 N (down the incline)

Wy = mg cos 30° = 49 × 0.866 = 42.4 N (into the surface)

Step 5: Apply Newton's Second Law along each axis:

x: mg sin θ = max  ⇒  ax = g sin 30° = 9.8 × 0.50 = 4.9 m/s²

y: N − mg cos θ = 0  ⇒  N = mg cos 30° = 42.4 N

The block accelerates at 4.9 m/s² down the incline. Note that the normal force is less than the full weight—only the component of gravity perpendicular to the surface is balanced by N.

Free body diagrams may seem tedious for simple problems, but they become absolutely essential as problems grow more complex. Systems with multiple objects, pulleys, ropes at angles, and friction all require careful FBDs to avoid errors. The habit of always drawing a free body diagram before writing any equations is one of the most valuable skills you can develop in physics.

Weight and Normal Force

Two of the most frequently encountered forces in mechanics are weight and the normal force. Despite their familiarity, students often confuse them or assume they are always equal. Let us examine each carefully.

Weight is the gravitational force exerted on an object by a planet (usually the Earth). Near the Earth's surface, the weight of an object of mass m is:

Weight

W = mg

Here g = 9.8 m/s² is the acceleration due to gravity near Earth's surface. Weight is a force (measured in newtons), not a mass (measured in kilograms). Weight always points straight down toward the center of the Earth.

It is essential to distinguish weight from mass. Mass is an intrinsic property of an object—it does not change when you travel to the Moon. Weight, however, depends on the local gravitational field strength. On the Moon, where g is about 1.6 m/s², a 70 kg astronaut weighs only about 112 N, compared to 686 N on Earth. The astronaut's mass is 70 kg in both locations.

The normal force is a contact force exerted by a surface on an object resting on (or pressed against) that surface. "Normal" here means perpendicular—the normal force is always perpendicular to the surface of contact. It is a reactive force: its magnitude adjusts automatically to prevent the object from passing through the surface. If you place a book on a table, the table pushes up on the book with whatever force is needed to keep the book from falling through. If you push down on the book, the normal force increases to match.

A critical point: the normal force is not always equal to the weight. It equals the weight only when the object is on a horizontal surface with no other vertical forces and no vertical acceleration. The moment any of these conditions change, N ≠ mg.

Example: Apparent Weight in an Elevator

A 60 kg person stands on a bathroom scale inside an elevator. The scale reads the normal force (which is what we perceive as our "apparent weight"). Find the scale reading when the elevator:

(a) Accelerates upward at 2.0 m/s²:

Taking upward as positive, Newton's Second Law in the vertical direction gives:

N − mg = ma  ⇒  N = m(g + a) = 60(9.8 + 2.0) = 708 N

The person feels heavier than normal (their actual weight is only 588 N).

(b) Accelerates downward at 2.0 m/s²:

N − mg = m(−a)  ⇒  N = m(g − a) = 60(9.8 − 2.0) = 468 N

The person feels lighter. If the elevator were in free fall (a = g), the scale would read zero—this is the state of weightlessness experienced by astronauts in orbit (who are in continuous free fall around the Earth).

(c) Moves at constant velocity (up or down):

a = 0  ⇒  N = mg = 588 N

When the velocity is constant (including zero), the scale reads the true weight. This is another manifestation of the First Law.

The elevator example beautifully illustrates how the normal force is not simply "equal and opposite to gravity." The normal force is determined by Newton's Second Law applied to the specific situation. On an incline, the normal force equals mg cos θ. In an accelerating elevator, it equals m(g ± a). Always use ΣF = ma to find N—never assume it equals mg.

Friction

Friction is a contact force that opposes the relative motion (or attempted motion) between two surfaces in contact. It arises from the microscopic roughness of surfaces: tiny peaks and valleys interlock and must be broken apart for sliding to occur. Without friction, you could not walk, drive, or even pick up a glass of water. Yet in many engineering contexts, friction is an unwanted source of energy loss, and engineers work hard to minimize it.

There are two distinct types of friction that we encounter in introductory mechanics:

Static friction (fs) acts on an object that is not sliding relative to the surface. It is a self-adjusting force: if you gently push a heavy crate, static friction pushes back with exactly enough force to prevent motion. Push a little harder, and static friction increases to match. But there is a limit—static friction can only grow so large before the object begins to slide. This maximum value depends on how hard the surfaces are pressed together (the normal force) and the nature of the surfaces (characterized by the coefficient of static friction, μs):

Static Friction

fs ≤ μs N

Note the inequality. Static friction is at most μsN. Its actual value is whatever is needed to prevent sliding, up to this maximum. Only when the applied force exceeds μsN does the object begin to move.

Kinetic friction (fk) acts on an object that is already sliding across a surface. Unlike static friction, kinetic friction has a fixed magnitude (for a given normal force and pair of surfaces) and always opposes the direction of sliding:

Kinetic Friction

fk = μk N

Note the equality (not an inequality). Once the object is sliding, the friction force is constant at μkN. Typically, μk < μs, which is why it takes more force to start an object sliding than to keep it sliding.

The coefficients of friction μs and μk are dimensionless numbers that characterize the roughness of the surface pair. Rubber on dry concrete might have μs ≈ 1.0, while steel on ice might have μk ≈ 0.03. These coefficients are determined experimentally and can be found in reference tables.

Example: Moving a Heavy Crate

A worker pushes horizontally on a 40 kg wooden crate resting on a concrete floor. The coefficients of friction are μs = 0.50 and μk = 0.35. (a) What minimum force is needed to start the crate moving? (b) Once sliding, what force keeps it moving at constant velocity?

(a) Starting the crate:

The normal force on the crate equals its weight (horizontal surface, no vertical acceleration):

N = mg = 40 × 9.8 = 392 N

The crate begins to slide when the applied force exceeds maximum static friction:

Fmin = μs N = 0.50 × 392 = 196 N

(b) Constant velocity while sliding:

Constant velocity means zero acceleration, so the applied force must exactly equal kinetic friction:

F = fk = μk N = 0.35 × 392 = 137 N

Notice the applied force needed drops from 196 N to 137 N once the crate starts sliding—this is the practical consequence of μk < μs. This is why a stuck car sometimes lurches forward once you finally get the tires to spin: the transition from static to kinetic friction reduces the opposing force.

A few important caveats about the friction model: the equations above are approximations. Real friction depends on speed, surface area, temperature, and other factors that our simple model ignores. For most introductory problems, however, the model works well. Also, friction always acts parallel to the surface of contact and opposite to the direction of motion (or attempted motion)—never perpendicular to the surface (that is the normal force's job).

Tension

Tension is the pulling force transmitted through a string, rope, cable, or chain when it is pulled taut by forces acting from opposite ends. When you pull a dog on a leash, the tension in the leash transmits your pull to the dog. When a crane lifts a steel beam with a cable, the tension in the cable supports the beam's weight.

In introductory physics, we almost always model ropes and strings as ideal: they are massless and inextensible (they do not stretch). These assumptions have two important consequences:

First, if the rope is massless, the tension is the same at every point along the rope. To see why, imagine isolating a small segment of the rope. If the segment has zero mass, then by Newton's Second Law (ΣF = ma = 0 · a = 0), the net force on it must be zero. This means the pull from one side must equal the pull from the other side. Therefore the tension does not change along the length of the rope.

Second, if the rope is inextensible, then all objects connected by the rope move together—they have the same speed and the same magnitude of acceleration (though possibly in different directions if the rope passes over a pulley).

Tension always pulls; a rope can never push. If you try to push something with a rope, it simply goes slack. The direction of the tension force on any object is always along the rope, away from the object and toward the other end of the rope.

Example: The Atwood Machine

An Atwood machine consists of two masses, m1 = 5.0 kg and m2 = 3.0 kg, connected by a massless, inextensible string over a massless, frictionless pulley. Find the acceleration of the system and the tension in the string.

Setup: Since m1 > m2, m1 descends and m2 ascends. Both have the same magnitude of acceleration a. The tension T is the same throughout the string.

FBD for m1 (taking downward as positive for m1):

m1g − T = m1a   … (1)

FBD for m2 (taking upward as positive for m2):

T − m2g = m2a   … (2)

Solving: Add equations (1) and (2) to eliminate T:

m1g − m2g = (m1 + m2)a

a = (m1 − m2)g / (m1 + m2) = (5.0 − 3.0)(9.8) / (5.0 + 3.0) = 2.45 m/s²

For tension, substitute back into equation (2):

T = m2(g + a) = 3.0(9.8 + 2.45) = 36.75 N

Note that the tension (36.75 N) is between the two weights (m2g = 29.4 N and m1g = 49.0 N). This makes physical sense: the string must support m2 against gravity and accelerate it upward, but it must also allow m1 to accelerate downward, so it pulls upward on m1 with less than m1's full weight.

The Atwood machine is one of the most important systems in introductory mechanics. It elegantly demonstrates how to apply Newton's Second Law to a system of connected objects, how tension transmits force through a string, and how the constraint of a single string forces both masses to share the same acceleration magnitude. We will encounter similar techniques repeatedly when analyzing systems with pulleys, ropes, and connected bodies.

Applications

Let us now bring together everything we have learned—Newton's three laws, free body diagrams, weight, normal force, friction, and tension—in a comprehensive worked example involving connected objects.

Worked Example: Two Connected Blocks

A block of mass m1 = 8.0 kg sits on a horizontal table with coefficient of kinetic friction μk = 0.25. It is connected by a massless string over a frictionless pulley at the table's edge to a hanging block of mass m2 = 5.0 kg. The system is released from rest. Find (a) the acceleration and (b) the tension in the string.

Step 1: Draw separate FBDs for each block.

Block 1 (on the table): Weight m1g downward, normal force N upward, friction fk to the left (opposing motion), tension T to the right (toward the pulley).

Block 2 (hanging): Weight m2g downward, tension T upward.

Step 2: Write Newton's Second Law for each block.

Block 1 (horizontal direction, taking rightward as positive):

T − fk = m1a   … (1)

Block 1 (vertical direction): N − m1g = 0, so N = m1g = 78.4 N.

Therefore: fk = μkN = 0.25 × 78.4 = 19.6 N.

Block 2 (vertical direction, taking downward as positive):

m2g − T = m2a   … (2)

Step 3: Solve the system of equations.

Add equations (1) and (2):

m2g − fk = (m1 + m2)a

a = (m2g − μkm1g) / (m1 + m2)

a = (5.0 × 9.8 − 0.25 × 8.0 × 9.8) / (8.0 + 5.0)

a = (49.0 − 19.6) / 13.0 = 29.4 / 13.0 = 2.26 m/s²

Step 4: Find the tension.

Substitute into equation (2):

T = m2g − m2a = m2(g − a) = 5.0(9.8 − 2.26) = 37.7 N

Step 5: Check the answer.

Verify with equation (1): T − fk = 37.7 − 19.6 = 18.1 N. m1a = 8.0 × 2.26 = 18.1 N. The equations are consistent.

Also check physical reasonableness: the acceleration (2.26 m/s²) is less than g, which makes sense because friction on block 1 opposes the motion. The tension (37.7 N) is less than m2g (49.0 N), which makes sense because block 2 must accelerate downward. Everything checks out.

This problem illustrates a general strategy for connected-object problems: (1) draw a free body diagram for each object, (2) write ΣF = ma for each object along the direction of motion, (3) use the constraint that the string connects the objects (same magnitude of acceleration, same tension), and (4) solve the resulting system of equations. This approach generalizes to any number of connected objects.

Problems involving inclined planes with friction follow a similar approach but require careful resolution of weight into components parallel and perpendicular to the incline. Problems with multiple pulleys require tracking how the string constraint relates the accelerations of different masses. In every case, the method is the same: isolate each object, draw its FBD, apply Newton's Second Law, and solve.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception: "A force is needed to keep an object moving"

This is the Aristotelian view that Newton's First Law explicitly overturns. In the absence of friction and air resistance, an object in motion continues moving at constant velocity forever with no force applied. When you stop pushing a box and it stops, it is friction that brings it to rest, not the absence of a forward force. In outer space, a thrown ball would travel in a straight line at constant speed indefinitely.

Misconception: "Heavier objects fall faster"

In a vacuum, all objects fall with the same acceleration g regardless of mass. Newton's Second Law explains why: the gravitational force on an object is F = mg, so the acceleration is a = F/m = mg/m = g. The mass cancels. In the real world, air resistance affects lighter objects more (relative to their weight), which is why a feather falls more slowly than a hammer in air. But on the Moon, where there is no air, Apollo 15 astronaut David Scott demonstrated that a hammer and a feather hit the ground at exactly the same time.

Misconception: "The normal force always equals the weight"

As we saw in the elevator example, the normal force equals the weight only in the special case of a horizontal surface with no vertical acceleration and no other vertical forces. On an incline, N = mg cos θ (less than mg). In an upward-accelerating elevator, N = m(g + a) (greater than mg). If someone pushes down on a box sitting on a table, N = mg + Fpush (greater than mg). Always use Newton's Second Law to determine the normal force—never assume N = mg.

Misconception: "Action-reaction forces cancel out"

Third Law pairs never cancel because they act on different objects. Forces cancel only when they act on the same object. If you push a wall and the wall pushes you back, these forces do not cancel because one acts on the wall and the other acts on you. To determine whether an object accelerates, you must look at the net force on that particular object alone, which includes only the forces acting on it—not the forces it exerts on others.

Key Takeaways

  • A force is an interaction between two objects that can cause acceleration. It is a vector quantity measured in newtons (N).
  • Newton's First Law: An object remains at rest or in uniform straight-line motion unless a net external force acts on it. Force causes changes in motion, not motion itself.
  • Newton's Second Law: ΣF = ma. The net force determines the acceleration's magnitude and direction. Mass is the measure of an object's resistance to acceleration (inertia).
  • Newton's Third Law: Forces always come in equal-and-opposite pairs acting on different objects. These pairs never cancel each other because they are on separate objects.
  • Free body diagrams are essential. Isolate one object, draw all forces acting on it, choose coordinates, and apply ΣF = ma. This is the core method of Newtonian mechanics.
  • Weight (W = mg) is the gravitational force on an object, always directed downward. Normal force is perpendicular to the contact surface and is determined by Newton's Second Law, not simply set equal to mg.
  • Static friction (fs ≤ μsN) prevents sliding and self-adjusts up to a maximum. Kinetic friction (fk = μkN) opposes sliding with a fixed magnitude. Typically μk < μs.
  • Tension in an ideal (massless, inextensible) rope is constant throughout and always pulls. Connected objects share the same acceleration magnitude.
  • For connected-object problems, draw separate FBDs for each object, write ΣF = ma for each, use constraints (same string = same acceleration magnitude), and solve the system of equations.
  • Always check your answers for physical reasonableness: Are the signs correct? Is the acceleration less than g? Is tension between the relevant weights? Do the equations agree?
