Learning Goals

  • Define linear momentum and understand its vector nature
  • Define impulse and relate it to the change in momentum
  • Derive and apply the impulse-momentum theorem
  • Understand the law of conservation of momentum and derive it from Newton's third law
  • Distinguish between elastic, inelastic, and perfectly inelastic collisions
  • Solve collision problems using conservation of momentum and, where applicable, conservation of kinetic energy
  • Extend momentum conservation to two-dimensional collision problems
  • Identify and correct common misconceptions about momentum and collisions

Overview

In the previous lecture, we explored energy: a scalar quantity that is conserved and transformed. Energy gave us a powerful tool for analyzing motion without tracking forces at every instant. In this lecture, we introduce another conserved quantity that is equally fundamental but fundamentally different in character: momentum.

Momentum is a vector. Unlike energy, it has direction. And unlike energy, which can be converted between kinetic and potential forms, momentum in a closed system is simply conserved—the total momentum before an interaction equals the total momentum after, regardless of how violent or gentle the interaction may be. This makes momentum the ideal tool for analyzing collisions: two cars crashing, two billiard balls striking, a bullet embedding in a block, or even subatomic particles scattering off each other in a collider.

We will begin by defining momentum and then introduce impulse, which describes how forces change momentum over time. The impulse-momentum theorem connects these ideas and reveals why airbags save lives and why you bend your knees when landing from a jump. We will then derive the conservation of momentum from Newton's third law—showing that this conservation principle is not an additional assumption but a direct consequence of Newton's laws.

The second half of the lecture focuses on collisions. We will classify collisions into three types—elastic, inelastic, and perfectly inelastic—based on whether kinetic energy is conserved, partially lost, or maximally lost. For each type, we will develop the relevant equations and work through detailed examples. Finally, we extend these ideas to two dimensions, where momentum conservation must be applied separately along each axis.

By the end of this lecture, you will have two complementary tools for analyzing mechanical interactions: energy (from Lecture 5) and momentum (from this lecture). Many problems are most efficiently solved by using both simultaneously, and the ability to choose the right tool for the right situation is one of the hallmarks of a skilled physicist.

Linear Momentum

Imagine two objects moving at the same speed: a tennis ball and a freight train. Which one would be harder to stop? The freight train, obviously. It has vastly more mass. Now imagine two freight trains, one crawling at 1 km/h and the other barreling along at 100 km/h. The fast one is far harder to stop. What determines how difficult it is to change an object's state of motion is not just its mass or just its velocity, but the product of the two. This product is what we call momentum.

Linear Momentum p = mv
Linear Momentum (p)

The product of an object's mass and velocity: p = mv. Momentum is a vector quantity—it has the same direction as the velocity. The SI unit of momentum is kg · m/s. Momentum measures the "quantity of motion" and determines how much force or time is needed to change an object's state of motion.

Several features of this definition are worth emphasizing. First, momentum is a vector. It points in the same direction as the velocity. An object moving east has eastward momentum; if it turns around and moves west at the same speed, its momentum has reversed direction. This vector nature is essential for collision problems, where the direction of motion before and after the collision matters enormously.

Second, momentum depends linearly on both mass and velocity. Doubling the mass doubles the momentum. Doubling the velocity also doubles the momentum. Contrast this with kinetic energy, which depends on v²: doubling the velocity quadruples the kinetic energy but only doubles the momentum. This difference between the linear and quadratic dependence on velocity leads to situations where momentum is conserved but kinetic energy is not, and vice versa.

Third, an object at rest has zero momentum. This follows directly from the definition: if v = 0, then p = m(0) = 0. This is true regardless of the object's mass. A stationary freight train has zero momentum, just like a stationary tennis ball.

Newton himself recognized the importance of momentum. In fact, Newton's second law was originally stated not as F = ma but in terms of momentum. Newton wrote that the force acting on a body is equal to the rate of change of its momentum:

Newton's Second Law (Momentum Form) Fnet = Δp / Δt

For an object of constant mass, Δp = mΔv, so Δp/Δt = m(Δv/Δt) = ma, which recovers the familiar F = ma. But the momentum form is more general. It applies even when the mass is changing (as in a rocket expelling fuel) or when we are dealing with systems of particles. We will see why this form is so powerful when we derive conservation of momentum.

Key Concept

Momentum is the "quantity of motion" and determines how hard it is to stop or deflect an object. Newton's second law, in its most fundamental form, states that force equals the rate of change of momentum. This formulation is more general than F = ma and leads directly to the conservation of momentum.

Impulse

If force changes momentum, then the total change in momentum depends on two things: how large the force is and how long it acts. A small force acting for a long time can produce the same change in momentum as a large force acting for a short time. The quantity that captures this combined effect is called impulse.

Impulse J = FΔt = Δp
Impulse (J)

The product of the average force and the time interval over which it acts: J = FΔt. Impulse is a vector with the same direction as the force. It equals the change in momentum of the object: J = Δp. The SI unit of impulse is N · s, which is equivalent to kg · m/s (the same as momentum).

The relationship J = FΔt = Δp is simply a rearrangement of Newton's second law in momentum form. But writing it this way highlights something physically important: for a given change in momentum, there is a trade-off between force and time. If the time interval is large, the force can be small. If the time interval is small, the force must be large.

Airbags and Bending Your Knees

This trade-off is the physics behind many safety devices. Consider a car crash. When a car hits a wall and stops, the change in momentum is the same regardless of how the car stops. The car goes from some velocity to zero, and Δp = m(0) − mv = −mv. This change must happen no matter what.

But the force experienced by the occupants depends on the time over which this change occurs. If the car has a rigid front end and stops almost instantaneously (Δt is very small), the force is enormous and lethal. But if the car has a crumple zone that gradually deforms, extending the collision time to perhaps 0.1 seconds instead of 0.01 seconds, the average force is reduced by a factor of ten.

An airbag works on the same principle. As you decelerate, the airbag inflates and then gradually deflates as you press into it. This extends the time over which your momentum changes from several milliseconds (hitting the hard steering wheel) to tens of milliseconds (sinking into the airbag). The impulse is the same, but the force—and therefore the risk of injury—is dramatically reduced.

The same logic explains why you instinctively bend your knees when landing from a jump. By bending your knees, you increase the time over which your downward momentum is brought to zero. This reduces the force on your joints and bones. A gymnast performing a dismount is an expert at this: the deep knee bend upon landing is not a sign of weakness but a masterful application of impulse-momentum physics.

Key Concept

For a given change in momentum, increasing the time of interaction decreases the force. This principle underlies the design of crumple zones, airbags, helmets, padding, and the instinct to bend your knees on landing. Safety engineering is largely the art of managing the impulse-momentum trade-off.

The Impulse-Momentum Theorem

Let us state the impulse-momentum theorem formally. It is simply a restatement of Newton's second law, but its form is especially useful for collision and impact problems:

Impulse-Momentum Theorem Favg Δt = mvf − mvi

The left side is the impulse delivered to the object (the product of average force and time interval). The right side is the change in the object's momentum (final momentum minus initial momentum). The theorem says these are always equal.

This theorem is particularly useful when forces are large and act over short time intervals, as in collisions. In these situations, the exact time history of the force is often unknown or very complicated (the force rises sharply, peaks, and then drops to zero), but we can still find the average force if we know the change in momentum and the duration of the impact.

Worked Example

A 0.145 kg baseball moving horizontally at 40 m/s is hit by a bat. After the hit, the ball moves in the opposite direction at 50 m/s. The bat is in contact with the ball for 1.5 ms (0.0015 s). Find the average force exerted by the bat on the ball.

Solution

Choose the initial direction of the ball's motion as positive. Then vi = +40 m/s and vf = −50 m/s (negative because it reverses direction).

Δp = mvf − mvi = (0.145)(−50) − (0.145)(40)

Δp = −7.25 − 5.8 = −13.05 kg · m/s

Favg = Δp / Δt = −13.05 / 0.0015 = −8,700 N

The magnitude of the average force is 8,700 N (about 1,960 pounds). The negative sign indicates the force is in the direction opposite to the ball's initial motion, which makes sense: the bat pushes the ball backward. Notice how enormous this force is compared to the ball's weight (about 1.4 N). Collision forces are typically very large because the time interval is very small.

Conservation of Momentum

We now arrive at one of the most important principles in physics: the law of conservation of momentum. It states that for a system of objects with no external forces acting on it (an isolated system), the total momentum is constant:

Conservation of Momentum ptotal,before = ptotal,after

Or, for two objects:

Conservation of Momentum (Two Objects) m1v1i + m2v2i = m1v1f + m2v2f

Derivation from Newton's Third Law

This conservation law is not an additional postulate. It is a direct consequence of Newton's third law. Here is the argument. Consider two objects, 1 and 2, that interact with each other but experience no external forces. By Newton's third law, the force that object 1 exerts on object 2 is equal in magnitude and opposite in direction to the force that object 2 exerts on object 1:

F12 = −F21

Now apply Newton's second law in momentum form to each object:

F21 = Δp1 / Δt   and   F12 = Δp2 / Δt

Since F12 = −F21, we have:

Δp2 / Δt = −Δp1 / Δt

Therefore Δp1 + Δp2 = 0, which means:

Δ(p1 + p2) = 0

The total momentum of the system does not change. Whatever momentum object 1 gains, object 2 loses, and vice versa. Momentum is transferred between the objects, but the total is conserved. This derivation is beautifully simple and reveals why momentum conservation is such a robust principle: it rests on nothing more than Newton's third law.

Key Concept

Conservation of momentum is a direct consequence of Newton's third law. In any interaction between two objects, the momentum gained by one is exactly equal to the momentum lost by the other. No external force means no change in total momentum. This principle applies to all types of collisions—elastic, inelastic, or perfectly inelastic.

An important caveat: momentum is conserved only when no net external force acts on the system. In a collision between two cars on a road, the collision forces are internal to the two-car system (they are third-law pairs). But friction with the road is an external force. However, the collision typically happens so quickly (milliseconds) that friction has negligible time to change the system's momentum. So we can still apply momentum conservation during the brief collision interval, even though friction is present. This is a common and important approximation in collision problems.

Elastic Collisions

Now we turn to the classification and analysis of collisions. An elastic collision is one in which both momentum and kinetic energy are conserved. No kinetic energy is lost to heat, sound, deformation, or other forms. Elastic collisions are an idealization: no macroscopic collision is perfectly elastic. However, many collisions between hard objects (billiard balls, steel spheres) are approximately elastic, and collisions between atoms and subatomic particles can be truly elastic.

Elastic Collision

A collision in which both total momentum and total kinetic energy are conserved. No kinetic energy is converted to other forms. Elastic collisions represent the "ideal" case and are closely approximated by collisions between hard, rigid objects.

For a one-dimensional elastic collision between two objects, we have two conservation equations:

Momentum: m1v1i + m2v2i = m1v1f + m2v2f

Kinetic energy: ½m1v1i² + ½m2v2i² = ½m1v1f² + ½m2v2f²

These two equations, with two unknowns (v1f and v2f), can be solved simultaneously. The algebra can be simplified significantly by rewriting the kinetic energy equation. After some manipulation (factoring the difference of squares and using the momentum equation), we arrive at a remarkably elegant result:

Relative Velocity in Elastic Collisions v1i − v2i = −(v1f − v2f)

This says that in an elastic collision, the relative velocity of approach equals the relative velocity of separation (in magnitude, with a sign flip). The objects bounce apart at the same relative speed at which they came together. This is equivalent to conserving kinetic energy and is often easier to use in calculations.

Special Case: Equal Masses

When two objects of equal mass collide elastically head-on, and one is initially at rest, something beautiful happens. The moving object stops completely, and the stationary object moves off with the entire velocity of the first. You can verify this using the conservation equations: set m1 = m2 = m and v2i = 0, and you find v1f = 0 and v2f = v1i.

This is exactly what you see in Newton's cradle (the desk toy with hanging steel balls). When one ball swings in and hits the row, it stops, and the ball at the far end swings out with the same speed. Momentum and kinetic energy are both transferred completely from one ball to the other. This velocity exchange is a hallmark of equal-mass elastic collisions.

Worked Example

A 2 kg ball moving at 6 m/s collides elastically and head-on with a 4 kg ball at rest. Find the velocities of both balls after the collision.

Solution

Using momentum conservation: 2(6) + 4(0) = 2v1f + 4v2f
12 = 2v1f + 4v2f   ... (1)

Using the relative velocity condition for elastic collisions:
v1i − v2i = −(v1f − v2f)
6 − 0 = −(v1f − v2f)
v2f − v1f = 6   ... (2)

From (2): v2f = v1f + 6. Substitute into (1):
12 = 2v1f + 4(v1f + 6) = 6v1f + 24
6v1f = −12
v1f = −2 m/s

v2f = −2 + 6 = +4 m/s

The 2 kg ball bounces back at 2 m/s (the negative sign indicates reversal), and the 4 kg ball moves forward at 4 m/s. We can verify: total momentum before = 12 kg·m/s, after = 2(−2) + 4(4) = −4 + 16 = 12 kg·m/s. Kinetic energy before = ½(2)(36) = 36 J, after = ½(2)(4) + ½(4)(16) = 4 + 32 = 36 J. Both are conserved.

Key Concept

In elastic collisions, both momentum and kinetic energy are conserved. The relative speed of approach equals the relative speed of separation. For equal-mass elastic collisions with one object initially at rest, the objects exchange velocities completely.

Inelastic Collisions

Most real-world collisions are inelastic. In an inelastic collision, momentum is conserved but kinetic energy is not. Some kinetic energy is converted into other forms: thermal energy (the objects heat up), sound (you hear the crash), deformation (the objects dent or crumple), and internal energy. The total energy of the universe is still conserved, but mechanical kinetic energy alone is not.

Inelastic Collision

A collision in which total momentum is conserved but total kinetic energy is not. Some kinetic energy is transformed into thermal energy, sound, deformation, or other non-mechanical forms. Most real-world collisions are inelastic to varying degrees.

In an inelastic collision, we can still use conservation of momentum to analyze the problem. However, we cannot use conservation of kinetic energy, because KE is not conserved. This means that for a one-dimensional inelastic collision between two objects, we have only one equation (momentum conservation) but two unknowns (the two final velocities). We need additional information to solve the problem completely—for example, one of the final velocities, or the fraction of kinetic energy lost.

The amount of kinetic energy lost in an inelastic collision varies widely. Two rubber balls bouncing off each other might lose only a small fraction of their kinetic energy. Two cars colliding might lose a large fraction. The fraction lost depends on the materials, the geometry of the collision, and the speeds involved. In general, we define the coefficient of restitution, e, as the ratio of relative speeds after and before the collision:

e = |v2f − v1f| / |v1i − v2i|

For a perfectly elastic collision, e = 1 (the relative speed is unchanged). For a perfectly inelastic collision (objects stick together), e = 0. For all other inelastic collisions, 0 < e < 1.

Worked Example — Car Crash

A 1,500 kg car traveling east at 20 m/s collides with a 1,000 kg car traveling west at 15 m/s. After the collision, the 1,500 kg car moves east at 5 m/s. Find the velocity of the 1,000 kg car after the collision and the kinetic energy lost.

Solution

Choose east as positive. Then v1i = +20, v2i = −15.

Momentum conservation:
(1500)(20) + (1000)(−15) = (1500)(5) + (1000)v2f
30,000 − 15,000 = 7,500 + 1000v2f
15,000 = 7,500 + 1000v2f
v2f = +7.5 m/s (east)

KEbefore = ½(1500)(20)² + ½(1000)(15)² = 300,000 + 112,500 = 412,500 J

KEafter = ½(1500)(5)² + ½(1000)(7.5)² = 18,750 + 28,125 = 46,875 J

KE lost = 412,500 − 46,875 = 365,625 J

About 88.6% of the kinetic energy was lost, converted to heat, sound, and deformation of the vehicles. This is typical of car crashes, which are highly inelastic.

Perfectly Inelastic Collisions

A perfectly inelastic collision is the extreme case: the two objects collide and stick together, moving as a single combined object after the collision. This is the collision type that loses the maximum possible kinetic energy (consistent with momentum conservation). It is also the simplest to analyze, because the two objects share a common final velocity.

Perfectly Inelastic Collision m1v1i + m2v2i = (m1 + m2)vf

Since the two objects have the same final velocity vf, there is only one unknown, and momentum conservation alone is sufficient to solve for it.

Perfectly Inelastic Collision

A collision in which the two objects stick together and move as one unit after the collision. Momentum is conserved, but the maximum possible kinetic energy is lost (consistent with momentum conservation). The coefficient of restitution is zero.

The Ballistic Pendulum

One of the most elegant applications of perfectly inelastic collisions is the ballistic pendulum, a device historically used to measure the speed of bullets. A bullet of mass m is fired into a large block of mass M suspended by strings. The bullet embeds in the block (a perfectly inelastic collision), and the combined block-bullet system swings upward to some maximum height h. By measuring h, we can work backward to find the bullet's speed.

The analysis proceeds in two stages. First, during the collision (which happens very quickly), momentum is conserved but kinetic energy is not:

mvbullet = (m + M)vafter

Second, after the collision, as the block-bullet system swings upward, there are no non-conservative forces doing work (we neglect air resistance), so mechanical energy is conserved:

½(m + M)vafter² = (m + M)gh

From the second equation: vafter = √(2gh). Substituting into the first:

vbullet = [(m + M) / m] √(2gh)

Worked Example — Ballistic Pendulum

A 10 g bullet is fired into a 5 kg wooden block suspended as a pendulum. After the bullet embeds in the block, the combination swings upward to a height of 8 cm. Find the speed of the bullet.

Solution

m = 0.010 kg, M = 5 kg, h = 0.08 m

vafter = √(2gh) = √(2 × 9.8 × 0.08) = √1.568 = 1.25 m/s

vbullet = [(m + M) / m] × vafter

vbullet = [(0.010 + 5) / 0.010] × 1.25 = 501 × 1.25 = 626 m/s

The bullet was traveling at approximately 626 m/s. Notice how we used two different conservation laws for two different phases of the problem: momentum conservation for the collision (where KE is not conserved) and energy conservation for the swing (where momentum is not conserved, because gravity is an external force acting on the system during the swing).

Key Concept

The ballistic pendulum illustrates a crucial problem-solving strategy: use momentum conservation during the collision (when forces are large and internal) and energy conservation during the subsequent motion (when forces are conservative). Knowing which conservation law to apply at each stage is the key to solving multi-phase problems.

Two-Dimensional Collisions

So far, we have analyzed collisions in one dimension: objects moving along a single line. But many real collisions happen in two dimensions. Two billiard balls striking at an angle, a car T-boning another car at an intersection, or subatomic particles scattering in a collider—these all involve motion in a plane.

The key principle is that momentum is conserved as a vector. This means that the x-component of total momentum is conserved, and the y-component of total momentum is conserved, independently of each other. For a two-dimensional collision between two objects, we write two separate conservation equations:

Momentum Conservation in 2D x: m1v1ix + m2v2ix = m1v1fx + m2v2fx
y: m1v1iy + m2v2iy = m1v1fy + m2v2fy

Each equation is applied independently. The x-equation involves only x-components of velocity, and the y-equation involves only y-components. There is no "mixing" between directions.

In practice, solving a 2D collision problem often involves choosing a coordinate system wisely. A common choice is to align the x-axis with the initial velocity of one of the objects. Then the initial y-component of that object's velocity is zero, simplifying the equations. After the collision, the objects generally move at angles to the x-axis, and trigonometry is used to decompose their velocities into components.

Glancing Collisions

A particularly common type of 2D collision is the glancing collision, where one object (say a billiard ball) strikes another at an angle rather than head-on. If the target object is initially at rest, the two objects move off at different angles after the collision. For an elastic glancing collision between equal masses (one initially at rest), a remarkable result holds: the two objects always move off at right angles to each other (90° apart). This can be proven using both momentum and kinetic energy conservation, and it is something you can observe on any billiard table.

Worked Example — 2D Collision

A 3 kg ball moving east at 8 m/s collides with a 3 kg ball at rest. After the collision, the first ball moves at 30° above the east axis. Find the speeds and direction of both balls (elastic collision, equal masses).

Solution

For elastic collisions between equal masses with one at rest, the balls move at 90° to each other. So the second ball moves at 30° below the east axis (since the first goes 30° above).

x-momentum: (3)(8) = 3v1fcos 30° + 3v2fcos(−30°)
Since cos(−30°) = cos 30° = 0.866:
24 = 3(0.866)(v1f + v2f)   ... (1)

y-momentum: 0 = 3v1fsin 30° − 3v2fsin 30°
Wait—this would give v1f = v2f only if both angles from the x-axis are equal. Let the first ball go at angle α = 30° above and the second at angle β below.

y-momentum: 0 = 3v1fsin 30° − 3v2fsin β
Since α + β = 90°, β = 60°.
v1fsin 30° = v2fsin 60°
v1f(0.5) = v2f(0.866)
v1f = 1.732 v2f   ... (2)

x-momentum: 8 = v1fcos 30° + v2fcos 60°
8 = 0.866 v1f + 0.5 v2f   ... (3)

Substituting (2) into (3): 8 = 0.866(1.732 v2f) + 0.5 v2f = 1.5 v2f + 0.5 v2f = 2 v2f
v2f = 4 m/s,   v1f = 1.732 × 4 = 6.93 m/s

The first ball moves at 6.93 m/s at 30° above east, and the second moves at 4 m/s at 60° below east. We can verify: KE before = ½(3)(64) = 96 J. KE after = ½(3)(48) + ½(3)(16) = 72 + 24 = 96 J. Conserved.

Key Concept

In two-dimensional collisions, momentum conservation is applied component-wise: separately in the x-direction and the y-direction. The two equations are independent and can be solved simultaneously. For elastic collisions between equal masses with one at rest, the objects always scatter at 90° to each other.

Common Misconceptions

Misconception

"A heavier object always has more momentum than a lighter one."

Not necessarily. Momentum depends on both mass and velocity: p = mv. A 0.01 kg bullet moving at 800 m/s has a momentum of 8 kg·m/s. A 100 kg person standing still has a momentum of zero. And a 1 kg ball moving at 10 m/s has a momentum of 10 kg·m/s, which is more than the bullet despite having much less mass. It is the product of mass and velocity that determines momentum, not mass alone.

Misconception

"Momentum is the same thing as kinetic energy."

Momentum (p = mv) and kinetic energy (KE = ½mv²) are related but fundamentally different quantities. Momentum is a vector; kinetic energy is a scalar. Momentum depends linearly on velocity; kinetic energy depends on v². Momentum can be negative (indicating direction); kinetic energy is always non-negative. In collisions, momentum is always conserved (for isolated systems), but kinetic energy is conserved only in elastic collisions. Two objects can have the same momentum but different kinetic energies, and vice versa.

Misconception

"In a perfectly inelastic collision, all kinetic energy is lost."

No. In a perfectly inelastic collision, the maximum possible kinetic energy is lost (consistent with momentum conservation), but not all of it. There is always some remaining kinetic energy unless the total momentum of the system is zero. If two equal-mass objects collide head-on at the same speed, their momenta are equal and opposite, the total momentum is zero, and they stick together at rest—in that special case, all kinetic energy is indeed lost. But in general, the combined object moves after the collision, and it has kinetic energy.

Misconception

"A larger force always causes a larger change in momentum."

Not if you do not account for time. The change in momentum (impulse) is J = FΔt. A very large force acting for a very short time might produce the same impulse as a small force acting for a long time. The magnitude of the force alone does not determine the change in momentum—the duration matters equally. This is precisely why airbags work: they reduce the force by increasing the time, while the impulse (change in momentum) remains the same.

Key Takeaways

  • Linear momentum, p = mv, is a vector quantity that describes the "quantity of motion." It has the same direction as the velocity and is measured in kg · m/s.
  • Impulse, J = FΔt, equals the change in momentum. For a given Δp, increasing the interaction time decreases the force, which is the basis for airbags, helmets, and crumple zones.
  • Newton's second law in momentum form, F = Δp/Δt, is the most general statement of the law. It reduces to F = ma for constant-mass systems.
  • Conservation of momentum is derived directly from Newton's third law. In any isolated system, total momentum is constant: pbefore = pafter.
  • In elastic collisions, both momentum and kinetic energy are conserved. The relative speed of approach equals the relative speed of separation. For equal-mass elastic collisions with one object at rest, the objects exchange velocities.
  • In inelastic collisions, momentum is conserved but kinetic energy is not. The lost kinetic energy is converted to heat, sound, and deformation.
  • In perfectly inelastic collisions, the objects stick together. Momentum is conserved, and the maximum kinetic energy is lost consistent with momentum conservation.
  • The ballistic pendulum illustrates using momentum conservation during the collision and energy conservation during the subsequent swing—a two-phase analysis strategy.
  • In two-dimensional collisions, momentum conservation is applied independently along each axis. For equal-mass elastic collisions with one object at rest, the objects scatter at 90° to each other.
